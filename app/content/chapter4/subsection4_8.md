# 4.8 Ratio Analysis (P/E, PEG, P/B, P/S)

## Overview
Financial ratios are powerful tools that help investors analyze and compare companies across different industries, sizes, and time periods. They distill complex financial information into simple metrics that provide insights into a company's valuation, profitability, efficiency, and financial health. This subsection focuses on four key valuation ratios: Price-to-Earnings (P/E), Price/Earnings-to-Growth (PEG), Price-to-Book (P/B), and Price-to-Sales (P/S).

## P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

### Definition
The Price-to-Earnings ratio is one of the most widely used valuation metrics. It compares a company's current share price to its earnings per share (EPS):

```
P/E Ratio = Current Share Price ÷ Earnings Per Share
```

### Types of P/E Ratios
- **Trailing P/E**: Uses EPS from the past 12 months (historical)
- **Forward P/E**: Uses projected EPS for the next 12 months (forecast)
- **Adjusted P/E**: Excludes one-time events or non-recurring items

### Interpretation
- **High P/E**: May indicate high growth expectations, industry premium, or potential overvaluation
- **Low P/E**: May suggest undervaluation, low growth expectations, or industry challenges
- **Negative P/E**: Indicates the company is currently unprofitable (not typically reported)

### Limitations
- Doesn't account for growth rates
- Can be distorted by accounting practices
- Less useful for companies with inconsistent earnings
- Varies significantly across industries
- Doesn't consider debt levels or capital structure

### Practical Application
- Compare a company's P/E to its historical average
- Compare to industry peers and market averages
- Use in conjunction with other metrics for a more complete picture
- Consider the business cycle and interest rate environment

## PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)

### Definition
The PEG ratio adjusts the P/E ratio for a company's earnings growth rate, providing a more nuanced valuation metric:

```
PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio ÷ Annual EPS Growth Rate
```

### Types of PEG Ratios
- **Trailing PEG**: Uses historical growth rates
- **Forward PEG**: Uses projected growth rates
- **5-Year PEG**: Uses 5-year average growth rates for more stability

### Interpretation
- **PEG < 1**: Potentially undervalued relative to growth
- **PEG = 1**: Potentially fairly valued relative to growth
- **PEG > 1**: Potentially overvalued relative to growth

### Limitations
- Relies on growth projections, which may be inaccurate
- Doesn't account for risk or quality of growth
- Less useful for mature companies with low growth
- Doesn't consider dividend yield

### Practical Application
- Particularly useful for comparing growth companies
- Helps identify growth at a reasonable price (GARP) opportunities
- More informative than P/E alone for high-growth industries
- Consider the reliability of growth projections

## P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

### Definition
The Price-to-Book ratio compares a company's market value to its book value (assets minus liabilities):

```
P/B Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
```

Where:
```
Book Value per Share = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) ÷ Number of Outstanding Shares
```

### Interpretation
- **P/B < 1**: Trading below book value, potential value opportunity or distress
- **P/B = 1**: Trading at book value
- **P/B > 1**: Trading above book value, common for healthy companies

### Limitations
- Book value may not reflect the true value of assets
- Intangible assets (intellectual property, brand value) often undervalued
- Less relevant for service or technology companies with few tangible assets
- Accounting practices can affect book value

### Practical Application
- Traditionally used for financial institutions and asset-heavy industries
- Useful for value investing strategies
- Helps identify potentially undervalued companies
- Consider return on equity (ROE) alongside P/B

## P/S Ratio (Price-to-Sales)

### Definition
The Price-to-Sales ratio compares a company's market capitalization to its annual revenue:

```
P/S Ratio = Market Capitalization ÷ Annual Revenue
```

Or on a per-share basis:
```
P/S Ratio = Share Price ÷ Sales per Share
```

### Interpretation
- **Low P/S**: May indicate undervaluation or low profit margins
- **High P/S**: May indicate overvaluation or high profit margins
- Industry norms vary significantly

### Advantages
- Useful for unprofitable companies
- Less susceptible to accounting manipulations than earnings-based metrics
- More stable than earnings-based metrics
- Allows comparison of companies with different profitability levels

### Limitations
- Doesn't account for profitability or margins
- Doesn't consider debt levels
- Varies significantly across industries
- Revenue quality matters (recurring vs. one-time)

### Practical Application
- Particularly useful for early-stage or unprofitable companies
- Valuable for comparing companies within the same industry
- Consider alongside margin analysis
- Look at trends over time

## Comparative Analysis

### Cross-Ratio Analysis
Using multiple ratios together provides a more comprehensive view:

- **High P/E, Low PEG**: May indicate strong growth prospects
- **Low P/B, High ROE**: Potentially undervalued with good returns
- **Low P/S, Improving Margins**: Possible turnaround candidate

### Industry Context
Different industries have different typical ratio ranges:

- **Technology**: Often higher P/E, P/S due to growth expectations
- **Utilities**: Typically lower P/E, higher dividend yields
- **Financial**: Often evaluated using P/B ratio
- **Retail**: Commonly compared using P/S and margins

### Market Cycle Considerations
Ratios should be interpreted differently at various points in the market cycle:

- **Bull Markets**: Average ratios tend to be higher
- **Bear Markets**: Average ratios tend to be lower
- **Recovery Phases**: Forward-looking metrics gain importance

## Key Takeaways
- P/E ratio measures the price investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings
- PEG ratio adjusts P/E for growth, providing a more nuanced valuation metric
- P/B ratio compares market value to book value, useful for asset-heavy companies
- P/S ratio compares price to sales, valuable for unprofitable or early-stage companies
- No single ratio tells the complete story—use multiple ratios for comprehensive analysis
- Always compare ratios to industry peers, historical averages, and market conditions
- Consider qualitative factors alongside quantitative metrics
