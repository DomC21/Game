# 4.4 Understanding Key Metrics & Terms

## Overview
Before diving deeper into fundamental analysis, it's essential to understand the key metrics and terms that form the foundation of company valuation. These metrics provide a standardized way to evaluate and compare companies across different industries and market capitalizations. This subsection introduces the most important metrics that every fundamental analyst should know.

## Market Capitalization

### Definition
Market capitalization (or "market cap") represents the total market value of a company's outstanding shares of stock. It is calculated by multiplying the current share price by the total number of outstanding shares:

```
Market Cap = Current Share Price × Total Outstanding Shares
```

### Significance
Market cap indicates the size of a company and helps categorize companies into different segments:

- **Large-Cap**: Companies with market caps of $10 billion or more
- **Mid-Cap**: Companies with market caps between $2 billion and $10 billion
- **Small-Cap**: Companies with market caps between $300 million and $2 billion
- **Micro-Cap**: Companies with market caps below $300 million

### Practical Application
Market cap helps investors:
- Assess a company's stability and risk profile
- Determine appropriate peer groups for comparison
- Balance portfolio diversification across different company sizes

## EPS (Earnings per Share)

### Definition
Earnings per Share (EPS) represents a company's profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock. It is calculated by dividing the company's net income by its total number of outstanding shares:

```
EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) ÷ Weighted Average Outstanding Shares
```

### Types of EPS
- **Trailing EPS**: Based on earnings from the past 12 months
- **Forward EPS**: Based on projected earnings for the next 12 months
- **Basic EPS**: Calculated using only outstanding shares
- **Diluted EPS**: Accounts for all potential dilution from convertible securities

### Significance
EPS is one of the most closely watched metrics by investors because it:
- Indicates profitability on a per-share basis
- Forms the basis for the P/E ratio
- Provides insight into a company's earnings growth
- Often influences share price movements after earnings announcements

## Dividend Yield

### Definition
Dividend yield represents the annual dividend payment as a percentage of the current share price:

```
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividends per Share ÷ Current Share Price × 100%
```

### Significance
Dividend yield is particularly important for income-focused investors because it:
- Indicates the cash return on investment from dividends
- Helps compare income potential across different investments
- Can signal value opportunities when unusually high (or potential dividend cuts when extremely high)
- Provides a component of total return alongside capital appreciation

### Considerations
When evaluating dividend yield, investors should also consider:
- **Dividend Payout Ratio**: Percentage of earnings paid as dividends
- **Dividend Growth Rate**: Historical increase in dividend payments
- **Dividend Consistency**: Track record of maintaining or increasing dividends

## P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

### Definition
The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio measures a company's current share price relative to its EPS:

```
P/E Ratio = Current Share Price ÷ Earnings per Share
```

### Types of P/E Ratios
- **Trailing P/E**: Uses EPS from the past 12 months
- **Forward P/E**: Uses projected EPS for the next 12 months
- **Adjusted P/E**: Excludes one-time events or non-recurring items

### Significance
The P/E ratio is one of the most widely used valuation metrics because it:
- Indicates how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings
- Helps assess whether a stock is potentially overvalued or undervalued
- Facilitates comparisons between companies in the same industry
- Reflects market expectations about future growth

### Interpretation
- **High P/E**: May indicate high growth expectations or potential overvaluation
- **Low P/E**: May suggest undervaluation or concerns about future growth
- **Negative P/E**: Indicates the company is currently unprofitable

## Other Important Metrics

### Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio
Compares a company's market value to its book value (assets minus liabilities):
```
P/B Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
```

### Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio
Compares a company's market cap to its annual revenue:
```
P/S Ratio = Market Capitalization ÷ Annual Revenue
```

### Enterprise Value (EV)
Represents the total value of a company, including debt and excluding cash:
```
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt - Cash and Cash Equivalents
```

### EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization—a measure of operational profitability:
```
EBITDA = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization
```

## Key Takeaways
- Market capitalization represents a company's total market value and helps categorize companies by size
- EPS measures profitability on a per-share basis and is a key component of many valuation metrics
- Dividend yield indicates the cash return from dividend payments relative to share price
- The P/E ratio is a fundamental valuation metric that helps assess whether a stock is potentially overvalued or undervalued
- Understanding these key metrics provides a foundation for more advanced fundamental analysis
