# 3.9 Chart Patterns: Continuation

## Introduction
Continuation patterns are specific chart formations that signal a temporary pause in the prevailing trend before it resumes in the same direction. These patterns are crucial for traders to identify potential entry points during ongoing trends. This subsection explores the most common continuation patterns, their characteristics, how to identify them, and strategies for trading them effectively.

## Flags and Pennants

### Flag Patterns
Flags are short-term consolidation patterns that form after a sharp price movement (the flagpole) and represent a brief pause in the trend.

**Characteristics:**
- Rectangular shape formed by two parallel trendlines
- Forms after a steep price move (the flagpole)
- Usually lasts 1-3 weeks (shorter duration than most patterns)
- Volume typically decreases during formation
- Volume increases on breakout

**Bullish Flag:**
- Forms during an uptrend
- Parallel downward-sloping trendlines
- Represents a brief consolidation before uptrend continuation

**Bearish Flag:**
- Forms during a downtrend
- Parallel upward-sloping trendlines
- Represents a brief consolidation before downtrend continuation

### Pennant Patterns
Pennants are similar to flags but form a small symmetrical triangle pattern rather than a rectangular shape.

**Characteristics:**
- Triangular shape with converging trendlines
- Forms after a steep price move (the flagpole)
- Usually shorter in duration than triangles (1-3 weeks)
- Volume typically decreases during formation
- Volume increases on breakout

**Bullish Pennant:**
- Forms during an uptrend
- Converging trendlines after a sharp rise
- Breakout occurs to the upside

**Bearish Pennant:**
- Forms during a downtrend
- Converging trendlines after a sharp decline
- Breakout occurs to the downside

### Trading Flags and Pennants
**Entry Strategy:**
1. Identify the pattern and confirm the prevailing trend
2. Wait for a breakout in the direction of the trend
3. Enter on the breakout with confirmation (closing price beyond the trendline)
4. Alternative: Enter on a pullback to the broken trendline

**Stop-Loss Placement:**
- Place stop-loss below the lowest point of the flag/pennant (for bullish patterns)
- Place stop-loss above the highest point of the flag/pennant (for bearish patterns)

**Profit Targets:**
- Measured Move: Add the height of the flagpole to the breakout point
- This provides a minimum price objective for the continuation move

## Triangle Patterns

### Symmetrical Triangles
Symmetrical triangles form when price consolidates between converging trendlines, with neither buyers nor sellers gaining clear control.

**Characteristics:**
- Upper trendline slopes downward, connecting lower highs
- Lower trendline slopes upward, connecting higher lows
- Volume typically decreases during formation
- Breakout usually occurs 2/3 to 3/4 of the way through the pattern
- Can break in either direction, but often continues the prevailing trend

**Trading Symmetrical Triangles:**
1. Identify the pattern within the context of the prevailing trend
2. Wait for a breakout (preferably in the direction of the larger trend)
3. Confirm with increased volume and a decisive close beyond the trendline
4. Set stop-loss on the opposite side of the triangle
5. Target at least the height of the triangle added to the breakout point

### Ascending Triangles
Ascending triangles are bullish continuation patterns characterized by a flat upper resistance line and an upward-sloping lower support line.

**Characteristics:**
- Horizontal upper resistance line (connecting equal highs)
- Upward-sloping lower support line (connecting higher lows)
- Typically forms in uptrends
- Volume decreases during formation and increases on breakout
- Breakout usually occurs to the upside

**Trading Ascending Triangles:**
1. Identify the pattern in an uptrend
2. Wait for a breakout above the horizontal resistance
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss below the most recent swing low
5. Target at least the height of the triangle added to the breakout point

### Descending Triangles
Descending triangles are bearish continuation patterns characterized by a flat lower support line and a downward-sloping upper resistance line.

**Characteristics:**
- Horizontal lower support line (connecting equal lows)
- Downward-sloping upper resistance line (connecting lower highs)
- Typically forms in downtrends
- Volume decreases during formation and increases on breakout
- Breakout usually occurs to the downside

**Trading Descending Triangles:**
1. Identify the pattern in a downtrend
2. Wait for a breakout below the horizontal support
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss above the most recent swing high
5. Target at least the height of the triangle added to the breakout point

## Rectangle Patterns

### Characteristics of Rectangle Patterns
Rectangles (also called trading ranges or consolidation zones) form when price bounces between parallel support and resistance levels.

**Key Features:**
- Horizontal support line connecting at least two lows
- Horizontal resistance line connecting at least two highs
- Price oscillates between these two levels
- Volume typically decreases during formation
- Can break in either direction, but often continues the prevailing trend

### Bullish Rectangle
When a rectangle forms during an uptrend, it's considered a bullish continuation pattern.

**Trading Bullish Rectangles:**
1. Identify the pattern in an uptrend
2. Wait for a breakout above the upper resistance line
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss below the lower support line
5. Target at least the height of the rectangle added to the breakout point
6. Alternative strategy: Trade bounces within the rectangle while waiting for breakout

### Bearish Rectangle
When a rectangle forms during a downtrend, it's considered a bearish continuation pattern.

**Trading Bearish Rectangles:**
1. Identify the pattern in a downtrend
2. Wait for a breakout below the lower support line
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss above the upper resistance line
5. Target at least the height of the rectangle added to the breakout point
6. Alternative strategy: Trade bounces within the rectangle while waiting for breakout

## Cup and Handle Pattern

### Formation and Characteristics
The cup and handle is a bullish continuation pattern that resembles a teacup on a chart.

**Cup Formation:**
- Rounded bottom formation (the cup)
- Typically spans 1-6 months
- Depth usually 1/3 to 2/3 of the previous advance
- Volume decreases as the cup forms and increases as price approaches the previous high

**Handle Formation:**
- Short pullback after the cup is complete (the handle)
- Typically lasts 1-4 weeks
- Shallower than the cup (usually staying in the upper half)
- Volume decreases during handle formation
- Often forms a small flag or pennant pattern

### Trading the Cup and Handle
**Entry Strategy:**
1. Identify the pattern in an uptrend
2. Wait for the handle to form after the cup is complete
3. Enter on a breakout above the handle's resistance
4. Confirm with increased volume

**Stop-Loss Placement:**
- Conservative: Below the lowest point of the handle
- Aggressive: Below the midpoint of the handle

**Profit Targets:**
- Measured Move: Add the depth of the cup to the breakout point
- This provides a minimum price objective for the continuation move

## Wedge Patterns

### Rising and Falling Wedges
Wedges are similar to triangles but both trendlines slope in the same direction.

**Rising Wedge:**
- Both trendlines slope upward, with the lower trendline steeper
- Typically bearish when forming in an uptrend (reversal)
- Can be bullish when forming in a downtrend (continuation)
- Volume typically decreases during formation

**Falling Wedge:**
- Both trendlines slope downward, with the upper trendline steeper
- Typically bullish when forming in a downtrend (reversal)
- Can be bearish when forming in an uptrend (continuation)
- Volume typically decreases during formation

### Trading Wedge Patterns as Continuation
When wedges form as continuation patterns (against their typical reversal nature):

**Falling Wedge in Uptrend (Continuation):**
1. Identify a falling wedge during a pullback in an uptrend
2. Wait for a breakout above the upper trendline
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss below the lowest point of the wedge
5. Target at least the height of the wedge added to the breakout point

**Rising Wedge in Downtrend (Continuation):**
1. Identify a rising wedge during a rally in a downtrend
2. Wait for a breakout below the lower trendline
3. Confirm with increased volume
4. Set stop-loss above the highest point of the wedge
5. Target at least the height of the wedge added to the breakout point

## Measuring Continuation Pattern Targets

### The Measured Move Method
The measured move is a technique for estimating price targets after a pattern breakout.

**For Flags and Pennants:**
1. Measure the height of the flagpole (prior to the pattern)
2. Add this height to the breakout point for bullish patterns
3. Subtract this height from the breakout point for bearish patterns

**For Triangles and Rectangles:**
1. Measure the height of the pattern at its widest point
2. Add this height to the breakout point for bullish patterns
3. Subtract this height from the breakout point for bearish patterns

**For Cup and Handle:**
1. Measure the depth from the cup's rim to its bottom
2. Add this measurement to the breakout point

### Alternative Target Methods
**Fibonacci Extensions:**
- Use 127.2%, 161.8%, or 261.8% extensions from the pattern's range

**Previous Support/Resistance:**
- Identify key levels beyond the pattern that may act as targets

**Trendline Projections:**
- Extend trendlines to identify potential target areas

## Volume Analysis in Continuation Patterns

### Typical Volume Behavior
Volume provides important confirmation for continuation patterns:

1. **During Formation:**
   - Volume typically decreases as the pattern forms
   - Indicates decreasing interest during consolidation

2. **At Breakout:**
   - Volume should increase significantly
   - Confirms the validity of the breakout

3. **After Breakout:**
   - Volume should remain above average
   - Indicates sustained interest in the new move

### Volume Divergences
Watch for these volume warning signs:

1. **Low Volume Breakout:**
   - Breakout occurs with below-average volume
   - Suggests lack of conviction and potential false breakout

2. **Declining Volume After Breakout:**
   - Volume diminishes quickly after breakout
   - May indicate lack of follow-through

3. **Volume Spike Without Price Movement:**
   - High volume but price fails to break out
   - May indicate distribution or accumulation

## Key Takeaways
- Continuation patterns represent temporary pauses in the prevailing trend before it resumes
- Common continuation patterns include flags, pennants, triangles, rectangles, and cup and handle formations
- Volume typically decreases during pattern formation and increases on breakout
- The measured move technique helps establish price targets after breakouts
- Confirmation through volume, candlestick patterns, and indicators improves trading success
- Stop-loss placement should be based on the pattern's structure to manage risk effectively
