# 3.13 Divergence Trading

## Introduction
Divergence trading is a powerful technical analysis concept that identifies discrepancies between price action and technical indicators. These discrepancies often signal potential trend reversals or continuations before they become apparent on the price chart alone. This subsection explores the different types of divergences, how to identify them across various indicators, and strategies for trading them effectively.

## Understanding Divergence

### What Is Divergence?
Divergence occurs when the direction of the price trend and the direction of a technical indicator trend move in opposite directions. This disagreement suggests that the underlying momentum of the price movement may be weakening, potentially leading to a reversal or consolidation.

**Key Concept:**
- Price makes a new high/low, but the indicator fails to confirm with its own new high/low
- This non-confirmation suggests weakening momentum in the current price direction
- Divergences can appear on any oscillator-type indicator (RSI, MACD, Stochastic, etc.)
- They provide early warning signals before actual price reversals

### Types of Divergence
There are four main types of divergence:

1. **Regular (Classic) Bearish Divergence:**
   - Price makes a higher high
   - Indicator makes a lower high
   - Signals potential downward reversal

2. **Regular (Classic) Bullish Divergence:**
   - Price makes a lower low
   - Indicator makes a higher low
   - Signals potential upward reversal

3. **Hidden Bearish Divergence:**
   - Price makes a lower high
   - Indicator makes a higher high
   - Signals potential continuation of downtrend

4. **Hidden Bullish Divergence:**
   - Price makes a higher low
   - Indicator makes a lower low
   - Signals potential continuation of uptrend

### Divergence Strength and Significance
Not all divergences are created equal. Several factors influence their significance:

1. **Timeframe:** Divergences on higher timeframes (daily, weekly) are more significant than those on lower timeframes
2. **Duration:** Divergences that develop over longer periods tend to be more reliable
3. **Magnitude:** Larger discrepancies between price and indicator movements suggest stronger signals
4. **Multiple Indicators:** Divergences confirmed by multiple indicators increase reliability
5. **Market Context:** Divergences that align with key support/resistance levels or chart patterns are more significant

## Identifying Divergence on Different Indicators

### RSI Divergence
The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is one of the most popular indicators for spotting divergences.

**Characteristics:**
- Clear overbought (70+) and oversold (30-) boundaries make divergences easier to spot
- Smooth line makes trend direction in the indicator clear
- Works well in both trending and ranging markets

**How to Identify:**
1. Look for price making new highs/lows
2. Check if RSI is making corresponding new highs/lows
3. Draw trendlines on both price and RSI to highlight the divergence
4. Confirm with candlestick patterns or other technical signals

### MACD Divergence
The Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD) indicator can show divergences in multiple ways.

**Types of MACD Divergence:**
1. **MACD Line Divergence:** Between price and the MACD line
2. **Histogram Divergence:** Between price and the MACD histogram
3. **Signal Line Divergence:** Between price and the signal line

**How to Identify:**
1. Look for price making new highs/lows
2. Check if the MACD components are making corresponding new highs/lows
3. Pay special attention to the histogram, as it often shows divergence first
4. Confirm with other technical signals

### Stochastic Divergence
The Stochastic Oscillator is particularly effective for identifying divergences in ranging markets.

**Characteristics:**
- Clear overbought (80+) and oversold (20-) boundaries
- Fast-moving, which can provide earlier signals
- Can generate more false signals than slower oscillators

**How to Identify:**
1. Look for price making new highs/lows
2. Check if the %K line (and optionally %D) is making corresponding new highs/lows
3. Most reliable when divergence occurs in overbought or oversold territory
4. Confirm with other technical signals

### OBV Divergence
On-Balance Volume (OBV) divergence focuses on the relationship between price and volume.

**Characteristics:**
- Provides insight into the volume behind price movements
- Can confirm or contradict price action based on buying/selling pressure
- Not bounded by upper/lower limits like oscillators

**How to Identify:**
1. Look for price making new highs/lows
2. Check if OBV is making corresponding new highs/lows
3. OBV failing to confirm new price highs suggests weakening buying pressure
4. OBV failing to confirm new price lows suggests weakening selling pressure

## Trading Regular Divergence for Reversals

### Regular Bearish Divergence Strategy
This strategy aims to identify potential downward reversals in uptrends.

**Identification:**
- Price makes a higher high
- Indicator makes a lower high
- Most effective after extended uptrends
- More significant near resistance levels or overbought conditions

**Trading Approach:**
1. Identify the bearish divergence
2. Wait for confirmation (bearish candlestick pattern, trendline break, etc.)
3. Enter short position after confirmation
4. Place stop-loss above the recent swing high
5. Target previous support levels or use trailing stops

**Risk Management:**
- Avoid trading against strong uptrends without clear confirmation
- Use tighter stops in volatile markets
- Consider scaling into positions rather than full position sizing

### Regular Bullish Divergence Strategy
This strategy aims to identify potential upward reversals in downtrends.

**Identification:**
- Price makes a lower low
- Indicator makes a higher low
- Most effective after extended downtrends
- More significant near support levels or oversold conditions

**Trading Approach:**
1. Identify the bullish divergence
2. Wait for confirmation (bullish candlestick pattern, trendline break, etc.)
3. Enter long position after confirmation
4. Place stop-loss below the recent swing low
5. Target previous resistance levels or use trailing stops

**Risk Management:**
- Avoid trading against strong downtrends without clear confirmation
- Use tighter stops in volatile markets
- Consider scaling into positions rather than full position sizing

## Trading Hidden Divergence for Trend Continuation

### Hidden Bearish Divergence Strategy
This strategy aims to identify potential continuation of downtrends after pullbacks.

**Identification:**
- Price makes a lower high
- Indicator makes a higher high
- Most effective during established downtrends
- More significant when price pulls back to previous support (now resistance)

**Trading Approach:**
1. Identify the hidden bearish divergence
2. Wait for confirmation (rejection from resistance, bearish candlestick pattern, etc.)
3. Enter short position after confirmation
4. Place stop-loss above the recent swing high
5. Target previous lows or use trailing stops

**Risk Management:**
- Ensure the primary downtrend is still intact
- Avoid trading when price is showing signs of basing or reversal
- Use multiple timeframe analysis to confirm the larger trend

### Hidden Bullish Divergence Strategy
This strategy aims to identify potential continuation of uptrends after pullbacks.

**Identification:**
- Price makes a higher low
- Indicator makes a lower low
- Most effective during established uptrends
- More significant when price pulls back to previous resistance (now support)

**Trading Approach:**
1. Identify the hidden bullish divergence
2. Wait for confirmation (bounce from support, bullish candlestick pattern, etc.)
3. Enter long position after confirmation
4. Place stop-loss below the recent swing low
5. Target previous highs or use trailing stops

**Risk Management:**
- Ensure the primary uptrend is still intact
- Avoid trading when price is showing signs of topping or reversal
- Use multiple timeframe analysis to confirm the larger trend

## Advanced Divergence Concepts

### Multiple Timeframe Divergence
Analyzing divergences across multiple timeframes can provide stronger signals.

**Approach:**
1. Identify divergence on a higher timeframe (e.g., daily)
2. Look for confirming divergence on a lower timeframe (e.g., 4-hour)
3. Use the lowest timeframe (e.g., 1-hour) for entry timing
4. This multi-layered confirmation increases the probability of successful trades

**Benefits:**
- Filters out lower-probability setups
- Provides better context for the divergence
- Improves timing of entries and exits

### Multiple Indicator Divergence
Using multiple indicators to confirm divergence increases reliability.

**Approach:**
1. Identify divergence on one indicator (e.g., RSI)
2. Look for confirming divergence on a different indicator (e.g., MACD)
3. Prioritize trades where multiple indicators show divergence
4. Different indicators measure different aspects of price movement, so confirmation across indicators is powerful

**Effective Combinations:**
- RSI + Stochastic: Both momentum oscillators but with different calculation methods
- MACD + OBV: Combines momentum and volume analysis
- RSI + CCI: Different approaches to measuring momentum

### Divergence Failure Patterns
Not all divergences lead to reversals or continuations. Understanding failure patterns is important.

**Common Failure Scenarios:**
1. **Continuation Divergence:** Price continues in its original direction despite the divergence
2. **Weak Reversal:** Price reverses briefly but then resumes the original trend
3. **Sideways Movement:** Price moves into a consolidation phase rather than reversing

**How to Handle Failures:**
- Use strict confirmation criteria before entering trades
- Implement proper stop-loss placement
- Be prepared to exit quickly if the expected move doesn't materialize
- Learn from failures to refine your divergence trading approach

## Combining Divergence with Other Technical Tools

### Divergence with Support and Resistance
Divergences that occur at key support or resistance levels are particularly powerful.

**Approach:**
1. Identify important support/resistance levels on the chart
2. Look for divergences that form as price approaches these levels
3. Regular divergence at resistance in an uptrend is a strong reversal signal
4. Regular divergence at support in a downtrend is a strong reversal signal
5. Hidden divergence at support in an uptrend is a strong continuation signal
6. Hidden divergence at resistance in a downtrend is a strong continuation signal

### Divergence with Chart Patterns
Divergences can enhance the reliability of chart patterns.

**Effective Combinations:**
1. **Head and Shoulders with Divergence:** Divergence between the head and right shoulder confirms the pattern
2. **Double Top/Bottom with Divergence:** Divergence between the two tops/bottoms strengthens the reversal signal
3. **Triangle Breakouts with Divergence:** Divergence before a breakout may signal a false breakout
4. **Flags/Pennants with Divergence:** Hidden divergence during these continuation patterns confirms the primary trend

### Divergence with Candlestick Patterns
Combining divergence with candlestick patterns provides precise entry signals.

**Effective Combinations:**
1. **Bearish Divergence + Shooting Star/Evening Star:** Strong reversal signal at tops
2. **Bullish Divergence + Hammer/Morning Star:** Strong reversal signal at bottoms
3. **Hidden Bearish Divergence + Bearish Engulfing:** Strong continuation signal in downtrends
4. **Hidden Bullish Divergence + Bullish Engulfing:** Strong continuation signal in uptrends

## Common Mistakes in Divergence Trading

### Forcing Divergence
One common mistake is seeing divergence where it doesn't actually exist.

**How to Avoid:**
- Use clear, objective criteria for identifying divergence
- Draw trendlines on both price and indicators to confirm the divergence
- Avoid cherry-picking indicator peaks/troughs to fit your bias
- Remember that not every market move has a preceding divergence

### Trading Without Confirmation
Another mistake is entering trades based solely on divergence without waiting for confirmation.

**How to Avoid:**
- Always wait for price confirmation (candlestick patterns, trendline breaks, etc.)
- Use additional technical tools to confirm the signal
- Be patient and wait for the right setup rather than forcing trades
- Remember that divergence is a warning sign, not an immediate action signal

### Ignoring the Larger Trend
Trading divergence without considering the larger market context can lead to poor results.

**How to Avoid:**
- Always identify the primary trend before trading divergence
- Use multiple timeframe analysis to understand the broader context
- Give more weight to divergence signals that align with the larger trend
- Be more cautious when trading against the primary trend, even with divergence

## Key Takeaways
- Divergence occurs when price action and technical indicators move in opposite directions
- Regular divergence signals potential trend reversals, while hidden divergence signals trend continuations
- The most common indicators for divergence trading include RSI, MACD, Stochastic, and OBV
- Divergence strength depends on timeframe, duration, magnitude, and market context
- Always wait for confirmation before trading based on divergence signals
- Combining divergence with other technical tools increases trading success
- Multiple timeframe and multiple indicator approaches improve divergence trading reliability
- Understanding divergence failure patterns helps manage risk effectively
