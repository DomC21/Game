# 3.20 Intermarket Analysis

## Introduction
Intermarket analysis is a branch of technical analysis that examines the relationships and correlations between different financial markets to gain insights into potential price movements. Unlike traditional technical analysis that focuses on a single market in isolation, intermarket analysis recognizes that global markets are interconnected, with movements in one market often influencing or providing clues about potential movements in related markets. This subsection explores the key intermarket relationships, how they can be analyzed, and how traders can incorporate these insights into their trading strategies.

## Fundamental Intermarket Relationships

### The Four Major Market Groups
Intermarket analysis typically focuses on four primary market categories and their relationships:

1. **Stock Markets**: Equity indices, individual stocks, and sector ETFs
2. **Bond Markets**: Government bonds, corporate bonds, and interest rates
3. **Commodity Markets**: Energy, metals, agriculture, and other physical goods
4. **Currency Markets**: Major and minor forex pairs

**Traditional Relationships:**
- Bonds and stocks typically move in the same direction (both rise in economic expansion)
- Bonds and commodities typically move in opposite directions (inflation concerns)
- Commodities and the U.S. dollar typically move in opposite directions (dollar-denominated pricing)
- Strong domestic currency typically weakens exports and stock markets

**Changing Dynamics:**
- Relationships can shift during different economic regimes
- Central bank policies can temporarily alter traditional correlations
- Crisis periods often see correlations strengthen (risk-on/risk-off dynamics)
- Long-term structural changes can permanently alter relationships

### The Business Cycle and Intermarket Relationships
Different phases of the business cycle affect intermarket relationships:

**Early Expansion Phase:**
- Interest rates begin to rise from lows
- Bonds begin to weaken
- Stocks begin to strengthen
- Commodities begin to rise
- Yield curve steepens

**Late Expansion Phase:**
- Interest rates continue to rise
- Bonds weaken further
- Stocks continue to rise but at a slower pace
- Commodities show strong performance
- Yield curve begins to flatten

**Early Contraction Phase:**
- Interest rates peak and begin to fall
- Bonds begin to strengthen
- Stocks begin to weaken
- Commodities peak and begin to fall
- Yield curve inverts

**Late Contraction Phase:**
- Interest rates continue to fall
- Bonds show strong performance
- Stocks reach bottom and begin to stabilize
- Commodities remain weak
- Yield curve begins to normalize

### Correlation and Divergence
Understanding correlation and divergence is key to intermarket analysis:

**Correlation Types:**
- **Positive Correlation**: Markets move in the same direction
- **Negative Correlation**: Markets move in opposite directions
- **Zero Correlation**: No discernible relationship between markets

**Correlation Strength:**
- Perfect correlation: +1.0 (positive) or -1.0 (negative)
- Strong correlation: 0.7 to 1.0 (or -0.7 to -1.0)
- Moderate correlation: 0.3 to 0.7 (or -0.3 to -0.7)
- Weak correlation: 0.0 to 0.3 (or 0.0 to -0.3)

**Divergence Significance:**
- Divergence from established correlations often signals potential trend changes
- Temporary divergences may indicate short-term trading opportunities
- Persistent divergences may signal regime changes or structural shifts
- The longer and stronger the divergence, the more significant its implications

## Key Intermarket Relationships

### Stocks and Bonds
The relationship between stocks and bonds is one of the most watched intermarket relationships:

**Traditional Relationship:**
- Historically, stocks and bonds often moved in the same direction
- Economic growth benefited both asset classes
- Falling interest rates supported both stocks and bonds

**Modern Relationship:**
- Since the late 1990s, stocks and bonds have often moved in opposite directions
- Bonds now often serve as a "safe haven" during stock market declines
- The stock-bond correlation tends to be negative during periods of economic uncertainty

**Yield Curve Implications:**
- Steepening yield curve (long rates rising faster than short rates) traditionally bullish for stocks
- Flattening yield curve (short rates rising faster than long rates) often precedes economic slowdown
- Inverted yield curve (short rates higher than long rates) historically precedes recessions and stock market declines

**Trading Applications:**
- Use bond market strength/weakness as a confirmation or warning sign for stock positions
- Monitor yield curve shape for early warnings of economic regime changes
- Consider sector rotation based on interest rate movements (financial stocks vs. utilities)

### Commodities and the Dollar
The relationship between commodities and the U.S. dollar is typically inverse:

**Fundamental Reasons:**
- Most commodities are priced in U.S. dollars globally
- A stronger dollar makes commodities more expensive for foreign buyers, reducing demand
- A weaker dollar makes commodities cheaper for foreign buyers, increasing demand
- Commodity-producing countries' currencies often move with commodity prices

**Key Commodity-Currency Pairs:**
- Oil prices and the Canadian dollar (CAD)
- Gold prices and the Australian dollar (AUD)
- Agricultural commodities and the New Zealand dollar (NZD)
- Industrial metals and the Australian dollar (AUD)

**Trading Applications:**
- Use dollar strength/weakness to confirm commodity trades
- Monitor commodity prices for insights into related currencies
- Look for divergences between commodities and the dollar for potential trading opportunities
- Consider the impact of central bank policies on this relationship

### Crude Oil and Markets
Crude oil has significant relationships with various markets:

**Oil and Equities:**
- Traditionally, rising oil prices were negative for stocks (increased costs)
- In the modern era, the relationship is more complex:
  - Energy sector stocks typically rise with oil prices
  - Transportation and consumer discretionary stocks typically fall with rising oil prices
  - The overall market impact depends on whether price increases are demand-driven or supply-driven

**Oil and Currencies:**
- Positive correlation with currencies of oil-exporting nations (CAD, NOK, RUB)
- Negative correlation with currencies of oil-importing nations (JPY, EUR)
- The "petrodollar" system creates complex relationships with the USD

**Oil and Interest Rates:**
- Rising oil prices often lead to inflation concerns
- Inflation concerns typically lead to higher interest rates
- Higher interest rates can eventually dampen economic growth and oil demand

**Trading Applications:**
- Use oil price movements to anticipate sector rotation in equity markets
- Monitor oil for early inflation warnings that might affect interest rates
- Consider the impact of oil price shocks on different currencies
- Look for divergences between oil and related markets for potential trading opportunities

### Gold and Market Sentiment
Gold serves as both a commodity and a monetary asset, creating unique intermarket relationships:

**Gold and the Dollar:**
- Traditionally negative correlation (weaker dollar = stronger gold)
- Relationship can break down during crisis periods when both may rise as safe havens

**Gold and Real Interest Rates:**
- Strong negative correlation with real (inflation-adjusted) interest rates
- When real rates fall, gold typically rises
- When real rates rise, gold typically falls

**Gold and Equity Market Stress:**
- Often serves as a "fear gauge" or safe haven during market turmoil
- Tends to outperform during periods of equity market volatility
- Can provide portfolio diversification during market stress

**Trading Applications:**
- Monitor gold for early signs of market stress or inflation concerns
- Use gold/silver ratio as an indicator of economic confidence
- Consider gold miners as a leveraged play on gold prices
- Watch for divergences between gold and real interest rates

## Analyzing Intermarket Relationships

### Correlation Analysis
Correlation analysis provides a quantitative measure of intermarket relationships:

**Calculation Methods:**
- **Pearson Correlation Coefficient**: Measures linear relationship between two variables
- **Spearman Rank Correlation**: Measures monotonic relationship (direction but not necessarily linear)
- **Rolling Correlation**: Calculates correlation over a moving window to track changes over time

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Select the markets to analyze
2. Choose an appropriate timeframe (daily, weekly, monthly)
3. Calculate the correlation coefficient
4. Monitor changes in correlation over time
5. Look for significant deviations from historical norms

**Interpretation Guidelines:**
- Correlation is not causation
- Correlations change over time and across different market regimes
- Extreme correlation readings often revert to the mean
- Sudden correlation breakdowns may signal important market shifts

### Relative Strength Analysis
Relative strength analysis compares the performance of one market to another:

**Calculation Methods:**
- **Ratio Charts**: Divide the price of one market by another (e.g., SPX/TLT)
- **Relative Strength Line**: Plot the ratio over time to identify trends
- **Rate of Change Comparison**: Compare percentage changes over the same period

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Select the markets to compare
2. Create a ratio chart or relative strength line
3. Apply technical analysis to the ratio chart itself
4. Look for breakouts, trend changes, and divergences in the ratio

**Interpretation Guidelines:**
- Rising ratio indicates the numerator is outperforming the denominator
- Falling ratio indicates the denominator is outperforming the numerator
- Trend changes in the ratio often precede or confirm trend changes in the underlying markets
- Support/resistance levels on ratio charts can be significant

### Overlay Charts
Overlay charts visually compare price movements of different markets:

**Implementation Methods:**
- **Price Overlay**: Plot actual prices of multiple markets on the same chart
- **Normalized Overlay**: Convert prices to a common starting point (e.g., 100) for easier comparison
- **Percentage Change Overlay**: Plot percentage changes from a specific date

**Effective Practices:**
- Use different colors and line styles for clarity
- Consider using two y-axes for markets with different price scales
- Focus on pattern similarity rather than absolute price levels
- Look for leading/lagging relationships between markets

**Interpretation Guidelines:**
- Identify visual correlations and divergences
- Note which market tends to lead or lag
- Watch for pattern breakdowns that might signal relationship changes
- Consider the fundamental reasons behind visual relationships

### Intermarket Divergence
Intermarket divergence occurs when traditionally correlated markets move in unexpected directions:

**Types of Divergences:**
- **Positive to Negative**: Markets that typically move together begin moving in opposite directions
- **Negative to Positive**: Markets that typically move oppositely begin moving in the same direction
- **Strength Divergence**: Correlated markets continue moving in the same direction but with significantly different momentum

**Significance Factors:**
- Duration of the divergence
- Magnitude of the divergence
- Historical reliability of the relationship
- Fundamental factors supporting or contradicting the divergence

**Trading Applications:**
- Use divergences as early warning signs of potential trend changes
- Consider which market is more likely to "correct" to realign the relationship
- Look for confirmation from other technical indicators before acting on divergences
- Be aware that some divergences signal regime changes rather than trading opportunities

## Intermarket Trading Strategies

### Confirmation Strategy
This strategy uses intermarket relationships to confirm signals in the primary market:

**Methodology:**
1. Identify a potential trade setup in your primary market
2. Check related markets for confirmation or contradiction
3. Enter the trade only if related markets confirm the signal
4. Exit if intermarket relationships begin to contradict the trade

**Example:**
- Primary Signal: Bullish breakout in S&P 500
- Confirmation Checks:
  - Treasury yields rising (confirming economic optimism)
  - Cyclical sectors outperforming defensive sectors
  - Copper prices rising (confirming global growth)
  - VIX falling (confirming reduced fear)

**Risk Management:**
- Reduce position size if some but not all related markets confirm
- Use tighter stops if intermarket confirmation is weak
- Monitor related markets for early warning signs of reversal
- Consider the historical reliability of each confirmation relationship

### Divergence Strategy
This strategy trades potential reversals based on divergences between related markets:

**Methodology:**
1. Identify a significant divergence between traditionally correlated markets
2. Determine which market is more likely to "correct" to realign the relationship
3. Wait for technical confirmation in the target market
4. Enter position in the anticipated direction of realignment
5. Set stop-loss beyond significant support/resistance

**Example:**
- Divergence: S&P 500 rising while Treasury yields falling
- Analysis: Economic weakness suggested by bond market not reflected in stocks
- Trade: Short S&P 500 when technical indicators confirm weakness
- Confirmation: Breakdown below key support or bearish reversal pattern
- Stop: Above recent swing high

**Risk Management:**
- Use smaller position sizes for divergence trades
- Wait for technical confirmation before entering
- Consider options strategies to limit risk
- Be aware that divergences can persist longer than expected

### Relative Strength Rotation Strategy
This strategy involves rotating between markets or sectors based on relative strength:

**Methodology:**
1. Create ratio charts comparing different markets or sectors
2. Identify markets showing relative strength improvement
3. Allocate capital to the strongest markets or sectors
4. Regularly reassess relative strength and rotate as needed

**Example:**
- Compare sector ETFs to the broader market (XLF/SPY, XLK/SPY, etc.)
- Identify sectors showing improving relative strength
- Overweight those sectors in portfolio allocation
- Reduce exposure to sectors showing deteriorating relative strength

**Implementation Approaches:**
- **Trend Following**: Buy markets breaking out to new relative strength highs
- **Mean Reversion**: Buy oversold markets showing early signs of relative strength improvement
- **Momentum**: Focus on markets with the strongest rate of change in relative strength
- **Rotational**: Systematically rotate based on relative strength rankings

### Currency-Commodity Pairs Strategy
This strategy exploits the relationship between commodities and related currencies:

**Methodology:**
1. Identify strong trends in major commodities
2. Determine which currencies are most correlated to those commodities
3. Look for confirmation or divergence between the commodity and currency
4. Trade the currency based on the commodity trend and relationship analysis

**Example Pairs:**
- Crude Oil and Canadian Dollar (USD/CAD)
- Gold and Australian Dollar (AUD/USD)
- Copper and Chilean Peso (USD/CLP)
- Agricultural commodities and New Zealand Dollar (NZD/USD)

**Trading Approaches:**
- **Confirmation**: Trade the currency in the direction suggested by the commodity trend
- **Divergence**: Trade potential realignment when currency and commodity diverge
- **Leading Indicator**: Use commodity movements as leading indicators for currency trades
- **Carry Trade Enhancement**: Use commodity trends to select high-yield currencies with positive fundamentals

## Advanced Intermarket Concepts

### Intermarket Flow of Funds
Understanding how capital flows between markets provides deeper insights:

**Typical Flow Patterns:**
- **Risk-On Flow**: Money flows from bonds to stocks and commodities
- **Risk-Off Flow**: Money flows from stocks and commodities to bonds and safe-haven currencies
- **Inflation Hedge Flow**: Money flows from bonds to commodities and inflation-protected securities
- **Deflation Hedge Flow**: Money flows from commodities and stocks to high-quality bonds

**Monitoring Methods:**
- Track fund flows between different asset classes
- Monitor changes in institutional positioning
- Analyze changes in market capitalization across sectors
- Observe volume patterns during market transitions

**Trading Applications:**
- Anticipate which markets might benefit from capital outflows from other markets
- Position ahead of expected institutional rebalancing
- Identify potential market tops when flow patterns become extreme
- Look for early signs of regime changes in flow patterns

### Global Macro Considerations
Intermarket analysis must consider broader economic and policy factors:

**Key Macro Factors:**
- Central bank monetary policies
- Fiscal policy changes
- Global growth differentials
- Inflation/deflation dynamics
- Geopolitical developments

**Integration Approach:**
1. Identify the current global macro regime
2. Determine how this regime affects traditional intermarket relationships
3. Adjust correlation expectations based on the regime
4. Consider how policy changes might alter relationships
5. Monitor for signs of regime shifts

**Trading Implications:**
- Different intermarket strategies work better in different macro environments
- Regime shifts often create temporary dislocations and trading opportunities
- Long-term structural changes may permanently alter historical relationships
- Policy divergences between countries create opportunities in currency and bond markets

### Sentiment Indicators Across Markets
Comparing sentiment across markets provides valuable context:

**Cross-Market Sentiment Indicators:**
- VIX (equity volatility) vs. MOVE Index (bond volatility)
- Put/Call ratios across different markets
- Risk reversals in options markets
- Credit spreads vs. equity volatility

**Sentiment Extremes:**
- Extreme readings in one market may foreshadow moves in related markets
- Divergences in sentiment between related markets often precede price divergences
- Coordinated extreme sentiment across multiple markets often signals major turning points
- Sentiment normalization patterns often follow similar sequences across markets

**Trading Applications:**
- Use extreme sentiment in one market to anticipate potential moves in related markets
- Look for sentiment divergences as early warning signs
- Consider contrarian positions when sentiment reaches historical extremes across multiple markets
- Monitor the sequence of sentiment normalization across markets

## Key Takeaways
- Intermarket analysis examines relationships between different financial markets to gain trading insights
- The four major market groups are stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies
- Traditional relationships include the inverse relationship between bonds and commodities, and between commodities and the U.S. dollar
- Correlation analysis, relative strength analysis, and overlay charts are key tools for analyzing intermarket relationships
- Divergences between traditionally correlated markets often signal potential trend changes
- Trading strategies include confirmation, divergence, relative strength rotation, and currency-commodity pairs approaches
- Advanced concepts include understanding the flow of funds between markets and global macro considerations
- Intermarket relationships can change across different economic regimes and due to policy shifts
