# 5.19 Creating a Personal Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

## Introduction
An Investment Policy Statement (IPS) is a written document that clearly outlines an investor's goals, risk tolerance, time horizon, asset allocation strategy, and guidelines for portfolio management. While often associated with institutional investors, a personal IPS is equally valuable for individual investors. It serves as a roadmap for investment decisions, a framework for evaluating opportunities, and a safeguard against emotional reactions during market volatility. This subsection explores the components of a personal IPS, its benefits, and a step-by-step approach to creating one tailored to your unique circumstances.

## The Purpose and Benefits of an Investment Policy Statement

### Why Create a Personal IPS?

#### 1. Clarity and Focus
A personal IPS transforms vague financial aspirations into concrete, actionable goals:
- Articulates specific investment objectives with measurable targets
- Establishes clear priorities among competing financial goals
- Provides a framework for making consistent investment decisions
- Reduces the influence of noise, trends, and distractions

#### 2. Discipline During Market Volatility
One of the most valuable aspects of an IPS is its role as an emotional circuit-breaker:
- Serves as a pre-commitment device created during rational moments
- Provides objective criteria for decision-making during market stress
- Reduces the likelihood of panic selling during downturns
- Prevents performance chasing during market euphoria

#### 3. Consistency Across Time
An IPS promotes consistency in your investment approach:
- Maintains focus on long-term objectives despite short-term market movements
- Ensures continuity of strategy across different market environments
- Provides a framework for consistent evaluation of investment opportunities
- Reduces the impact of recency bias in decision-making

#### 4. Communication Tool
For those who work with financial advisors or have family members involved in financial decisions:
- Creates a shared understanding of investment goals and approach
- Establishes clear expectations for all parties
- Provides a reference point for discussions about strategy changes
- Ensures continuity if advisor relationships change

#### 5. Accountability Framework
An IPS creates a mechanism for self-accountability:
- Establishes clear criteria for evaluating investment performance
- Provides a basis for periodic portfolio reviews
- Creates a record of your investment philosophy and decisions
- Helps identify and correct drift from stated objectives

## Key Components of a Personal IPS

### 1. Personal Information and Purpose Statement

#### Basic Information
- Name(s) of the investor(s)
- Date the IPS was created
- Date of last review/update
- Purpose of the investment portfolio

#### Purpose Statement
A concise summary of why you're investing and what you hope to achieve:

**Example**:
"This Investment Policy Statement outlines the investment strategy for Jane Smith's retirement portfolio. The primary purpose of this portfolio is to accumulate sufficient assets to support a retirement income of $60,000 per year (in today's dollars) beginning at age 65 and lasting for at least 30 years."

### 2. Investment Goals and Objectives

#### Financial Goals
Specific, measurable, time-bound financial objectives:

**Examples**:
- "Accumulate $1.5 million (in today's dollars) by age 65 for retirement"
- "Generate $4,000 monthly income from the portfolio during retirement"
- "Provide $100,000 (in today's dollars) for each child's education by age 18"
- "Maintain an emergency fund equal to 6 months of expenses"

#### Return Objectives
Target returns needed to achieve your goals:

**Examples**:
- "Achieve a long-term average annual return of inflation plus 4%"
- "Generate income of at least 3% annually while preserving principal"
- "Maximize long-term total return within the constraints of the risk tolerance"

#### Priority Ranking
If you have multiple goals, establish their relative importance:

**Example**:
1. Retirement security (highest priority)
2. Emergency fund maintenance
3. Education funding
4. Legacy/inheritance goals (lowest priority)

### 3. Time Horizon

#### Overall Investment Timeframe
The broad time horizon for your investment strategy:

**Examples**:
- "Long-term (30+ years) for retirement portfolio"
- "Medium-term (10-15 years) for education funding"
- "Short-term (1-3 years) for home down payment"

#### Goal-Specific Timeframes
Specific time horizons for individual goals:

**Example**:
- "Retirement: 25 years until initial withdrawals, then 30+ years of distribution phase"
- "College Funding: 8 years until first withdrawal, 15 years until final withdrawal"
- "Home Purchase: 5 years until full withdrawal"

#### Liquidity Needs
Anticipated cash flow requirements:

**Example**:
- "No planned withdrawals for the next 10 years"
- "Need $25,000 in 5 years for down payment"
- "Plan to withdraw 4% annually beginning in 15 years"

### 4. Risk Tolerance and Capacity

#### Risk Tolerance Assessment
Your psychological comfort with investment volatility:

**Examples**:
- "Moderate risk tolerance: Comfortable with portfolio fluctuations of up to 15% in a given year"
- "Conservative risk tolerance: Prefer stability and income over growth potential"
- "Aggressive risk tolerance: Willing to accept significant short-term volatility for long-term growth"

#### Risk Capacity Evaluation
Your objective ability to withstand losses:

**Examples**:
- "High risk capacity: Stable income, minimal debt, adequate emergency reserves, long time horizon"
- "Moderate risk capacity: Stable income but significant fixed expenses, moderate time horizon"
- "Low risk capacity: Variable income, significant financial obligations, short time horizon"

#### Drawdown Tolerance
Maximum acceptable portfolio decline:

**Example**:
- "Maximum acceptable drawdown: 25% from peak value"
- "Would consider rebalancing if portfolio declines more than 15%"
- "Would reassess strategy if drawdown exceeds 30%"

### 5. Asset Allocation Strategy

#### Target Asset Allocation
Specific percentages for each asset class:

**Example**:
- Equities: 70%
  - U.S. Large Cap: 30%
  - U.S. Small/Mid Cap: 15%
  - International Developed: 15%
  - Emerging Markets: 10%
- Fixed Income: 25%
  - U.S. Investment Grade: 15%
  - International Bonds: 5%
  - High Yield: 5%
- Alternatives: 5%
  - REITs: 3%
  - Commodities: 2%

#### Allocation Ranges
Acceptable deviation from target allocations:

**Example**:
- Equities: 65-75%
- Fixed Income: 20-30%
- Alternatives: 0-10%
- Cash: 0-5%

#### Sub-Asset Class Constraints
Limits on specific investment categories:

**Example**:
- "Maximum 25% in any single sector"
- "Maximum 5% in any single emerging market country"
- "Maximum 20% in high-yield bonds"
- "Maximum 5% in any single stock position"

### 6. Implementation Strategy

#### Investment Selection Criteria
Guidelines for choosing specific investments:

**Examples**:
- "Prefer low-cost index funds and ETFs with expense ratios under 0.25%"
- "Select active managers only in less efficient market segments"
- "Prioritize tax efficiency in taxable accounts"
- "Minimum 5-year track record for active managers"

#### Account Location Strategy
Guidelines for which investments go in which accounts:

**Example**:
- "Hold tax-inefficient investments (REITs, high-yield bonds) in tax-advantaged accounts"
- "Hold tax-efficient investments (index funds, municipal bonds) in taxable accounts"
- "Prioritize Roth accounts for highest expected return investments"

#### Investment Restrictions
Specific limitations based on personal preferences:

**Example**:
- "No investments in tobacco, firearms, or gambling industries"
- "Preference for ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) investments"
- "No use of margin or leverage"
- "No alternative investments with liquidity restrictions exceeding 1 year"

### 7. Monitoring and Rebalancing Policy

#### Review Schedule
Frequency of portfolio reviews:

**Example**:
- "Comprehensive annual review of entire portfolio"
- "Quarterly review of asset allocation"
- "Monthly review of cash flow needs"

#### Rebalancing Triggers
Conditions that prompt portfolio adjustments:

**Examples**:
- "Rebalance when any asset class exceeds its allocation range"
- "Rebalance annually regardless of allocation drift"
- "Rebalance when any asset class deviates more than 5% from target"

#### Performance Evaluation Criteria
How investment results will be assessed:

**Example**:
- "Compare portfolio performance to a blended benchmark of 70% S&P 500, 30% Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index"
- "Evaluate performance over rolling 5-year periods"
- "Focus on risk-adjusted returns using Sharpe ratio"

### 8. Special Circumstances and Considerations

#### Tax Management
Strategies for tax efficiency:

**Example**:
- "Implement tax-loss harvesting when losses exceed $5,000"
- "Consider tax implications before any significant portfolio changes"
- "Target long-term capital gains treatment when possible"

#### Estate Planning Integration
Coordination with broader estate planning:

**Example**:
- "Consider stepped-up basis opportunities in estate planning"
- "Coordinate beneficiary designations with overall estate plan"
- "Review portfolio implications of planned charitable giving"

#### Special Financial Circumstances
Unique situations affecting investment strategy:

**Example**:
- "Concentrated position in employer stock to be reduced by 5% annually"
- "Anticipated inheritance of approximately $500,000 within 5-10 years"
- "Planned early retirement option at age 55"

## Creating Your Personal IPS: A Step-by-Step Approach

### Step 1: Assess Your Current Financial Situation

#### Take Inventory
- List all investment and retirement accounts
- Document current asset allocation
- Calculate net worth
- Review income, expenses, and savings rate

#### Identify Financial Strengths and Weaknesses
- Evaluate emergency reserves
- Assess debt levels and interest rates
- Review insurance coverage
- Identify potential financial vulnerabilities

#### Clarify Your Financial Values
- Reflect on what money means to you
- Identify your financial priorities
- Consider your comfort with risk and uncertainty
- Assess your desire for financial independence

### Step 2: Define Your Investment Goals

#### Identify Specific Objectives
- Retirement funding
- Education expenses
- Major purchases
- Legacy/charitable giving
- Other financial goals

#### Make Goals SMART
- **Specific**: Clearly defined outcome
- **Measurable**: Quantifiable target
- **Achievable**: Realistic given resources
- **Relevant**: Aligned with values and priorities
- **Time-bound**: Specific timeframe

#### Prioritize Multiple Goals
- Rank goals by importance
- Identify which goals have flexibility
- Determine which goals require dedicated funding
- Consider the consequences of not achieving each goal

### Step 3: Determine Your Risk Profile

#### Assess Risk Tolerance
- Complete a risk tolerance questionnaire
- Reflect on past behavior during market volatility
- Consider your emotional response to investment losses
- Evaluate your comfort with uncertainty

#### Evaluate Risk Capacity
- Consider your time horizon
- Assess your income stability
- Review your debt levels
- Evaluate your insurance coverage
- Consider your human capital (future earning potential)

#### Reconcile Tolerance and Capacity
- Identify any mismatches between risk tolerance and capacity
- Determine the more constraining factor
- Consider education to align tolerance with capacity
- Develop strategies to address gaps

### Step 4: Design Your Asset Allocation

#### Determine Appropriate Asset Mix
- Consider your risk profile
- Align with your time horizon
- Calculate required return to meet goals
- Review historical performance of various allocations

#### Establish Allocation Ranges
- Set minimum and maximum percentages for each asset class
- Consider wider ranges for less liquid asset classes
- Establish narrower ranges for core holdings
- Allow flexibility while maintaining discipline

#### Consider Sub-Asset Allocations
- Determine appropriate diversification within asset classes
- Consider factor exposures (value, growth, quality, etc.)
- Evaluate geographic diversification
- Assess sector weightings

### Step 5: Develop Implementation Guidelines

#### Establish Investment Selection Criteria
- Determine preference for active vs. passive management
- Set maximum acceptable expense ratios
- Establish minimum requirements for fund selection
- Consider tax efficiency requirements

#### Create an Investment Location Strategy
- Identify optimal placement for tax-efficient investments
- Determine appropriate investments for tax-advantaged accounts
- Consider future withdrawal strategies
- Optimize for tax diversification

#### Document Any Restrictions or Preferences
- List any industries or companies to avoid
- Document ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) preferences
- Note any concentrated positions to maintain or reduce
- Identify any legacy holdings with special considerations

### Step 6: Establish Monitoring and Rebalancing Protocols

#### Set Review Schedule
- Determine frequency of comprehensive reviews
- Establish regular check-ins for specific metrics
- Create calendar reminders for review dates
- Identify triggers for off-cycle reviews

#### Define Rebalancing Strategy
- Choose threshold-based or calendar-based approach
- Set specific rebalancing triggers
- Establish process for implementing rebalancing
- Consider tax implications of rebalancing

#### Select Performance Benchmarks
- Identify appropriate benchmarks for each asset class
- Create a blended benchmark for the overall portfolio
- Determine appropriate timeframes for performance evaluation
- Establish realistic performance expectations

### Step 7: Document and Implement Your IPS

#### Create a Written Document
- Compile all elements into a formal written statement
- Use clear, specific language
- Include dates and signatures
- Store in a secure but accessible location

#### Share with Relevant Parties
- Provide copies to financial advisors
- Share with spouse or partner
- Consider sharing with adult children or trustees
- Ensure accessibility in case of emergency

#### Implement the Strategy
- Align current investments with the IPS
- Make necessary account changes
- Establish automatic contributions
- Set up monitoring systems

### Step 8: Review and Update Regularly

#### Schedule Regular Reviews
- Annual comprehensive review
- Quarterly allocation check
- Life event-triggered reviews

#### Update for Life Changes
- Major life events (marriage, children, retirement)
- Significant income changes
- Health status changes
- Inheritance or windfall

#### Refine Based on Experience
- Adjust risk tolerance based on actual behavior
- Refine goals as circumstances change
- Update investment criteria based on market evolution
- Modify monitoring approach based on effectiveness

## Common IPS Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Excessive Complexity
- **Mistake**: Creating an overly detailed document that becomes unwieldy
- **Solution**: Focus on key elements that drive decisions; keep language clear and concise
- **Balance**: Include enough detail to be actionable without becoming overwhelming

### 2. Vague Guidelines
- **Mistake**: Using imprecise language that provides little practical guidance
- **Solution**: Include specific percentages, ranges, and triggers
- **Example**: "Rebalance when any asset class deviates more than 5% from target" vs. "Rebalance periodically"

### 3. Unrealistic Expectations
- **Mistake**: Setting return targets or risk parameters that are misaligned with market realities
- **Solution**: Base expectations on historical data and reasonable projections
- **Approach**: Consider multiple scenarios, including adverse outcomes

### 4. Inflexible Parameters
- **Mistake**: Creating rigid rules that don't allow for changing market conditions
- **Solution**: Include reasonable ranges and adaptation guidelines
- **Balance**: Maintain discipline while allowing thoughtful adjustments

### 5. Neglecting Implementation Details
- **Mistake**: Focusing on high-level strategy without practical implementation guidance
- **Solution**: Include specific investment selection criteria and account location strategies
- **Importance**: The best strategy is ineffective without clear implementation guidelines

### 6. Creating and Forgetting
- **Mistake**: Developing an IPS but never referring to it for decisions
- **Solution**: Schedule regular reviews and actively use the IPS for decision-making
- **Approach**: Keep the IPS accessible and reference it before making significant investment changes

## Key Takeaways
- A personal Investment Policy Statement (IPS) provides a structured framework for making consistent investment decisions aligned with your goals
- An effective IPS serves as an emotional circuit-breaker during market volatility, helping prevent impulsive decisions
- Key components include investment objectives, risk parameters, asset allocation strategy, implementation guidelines, and monitoring protocols
- Creating an IPS involves assessing your financial situation, defining goals, determining risk profile, designing asset allocation, and establishing monitoring procedures
- Your IPS should be reviewed and updated regularly, especially after significant life changes or shifts in financial circumstances
- Common IPS mistakes include excessive complexity, vague guidelines, unrealistic expectations, and neglecting implementation details
- Templates can provide a starting point, but your IPS should be customized to your unique situation and preferences
- The most valuable IPS is one that is actually used for decision-making, not simply created and filed away
