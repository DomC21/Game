# Chapter 5: Portfolio Construction & Asset Allocation

## Chapter Overview
Building on the foundations of trading/investing basics (Chapters 1–4), this chapter delves into how to construct and manage an investment portfolio. You'll learn the importance of asset allocation, the role of diversification in mitigating risk, and how to align your portfolio with your risk tolerance and financial goals. By the end, you'll be able to develop a basic yet robust investment policy that balances growth potential against potential drawdowns.

## Subsections

### 5.1 Introduction to Portfolio Management
Portfolio management is the art and science of selecting and overseeing a group of investments that meets an investor's long-term financial goals and risk tolerance. This subsection introduces key concepts including diversification, asset allocation, and rebalancing, focusing on the balance between risk and return rather than just picking individual stocks or funds.

### 5.2 The Risk-Return Trade-Off
This subsection explores the fundamental principle that higher potential returns usually come with higher risk. It helps readers understand their emotional and financial capacity to handle volatility or drawdowns (risk tolerance) and set realistic return expectations that align with their goals.

### 5.3 Diversification: Why It Matters
Diversification involves spreading investments across different asset classes, sectors, and geographic regions to reduce unsystematic risk. This subsection explains how diversification works, why it's important, and how correlation between assets affects diversification benefits.

### 5.4 Core Asset Classes & Their Roles
This subsection examines the main asset classes and their characteristics:
- Equities (Stocks): Growth potential with higher volatility
- Fixed Income (Bonds): Steadier income with typically lower volatility and returns
- Cash & Cash Equivalents: Liquidity and safety with minimal returns
- Real Estate: Physical property or REITs for income and potential appreciation
- Alternatives: Commodities, precious metals, hedge funds, private equity for further diversification

### 5.5 Asset Allocation Strategies
This subsection covers different approaches to asset allocation:
- Strategic Asset Allocation: Establishing and maintaining a long-term target allocation
- Tactical Asset Allocation: Making short-term adjustments based on market conditions
- Dynamic Asset Allocation: A more active approach with frequent adjustments in response to market shifts

### 5.6 Time Horizon & Investing Goals
This subsection explains how time horizon affects investment strategy, with longer horizons tolerating more volatility. It introduces goal-based investing for specific objectives (retirement, home purchase, education) and discusses liquidity requirements for different time frames.

### 5.7 Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)
This subsection introduces the core ideas of Modern Portfolio Theory, including the concept that combining assets with less than perfect correlation can maximize returns for a given level of risk. It explains the efficient frontier and discusses limitations of MPT.

### 5.8 Measuring Portfolio Risk
This subsection covers key metrics for measuring portfolio risk:
- Volatility (Standard Deviation): Measuring return variation
- Beta: Comparing portfolio volatility to a benchmark
- Value at Risk (VaR): Estimating potential loss over a set period with a given confidence level

### 5.9 Rebalancing Strategies
This subsection explores different rebalancing approaches:
- Calendar Rebalancing: Rebalancing on a set schedule
- Threshold (Percentage) Rebalancing: Rebalancing when allocations drift beyond set thresholds
It also discusses the pros (maintaining risk profile, enforcing discipline) and cons (transaction costs, tax implications) of rebalancing.

### 5.10 Factor Investing & Smart Beta
This subsection introduces factor investing (targeting specific factors like value, momentum, quality) and smart beta strategies (index strategies that deviate from traditional market-cap weighting). It also discusses the risks of factor strategies underperforming when factors go out of favor.

### 5.11 Sector & Geographic Diversification
This subsection covers diversification across different sectors (Technology, Healthcare, Energy, etc.) and geographic regions (Domestic, International, Emerging Markets), explaining how different sectors and regions have varying economic cycles and growth potentials.

### 5.12 Using Index Funds & ETFs
This subsection explains how index funds and ETFs can be used for portfolio construction, highlighting their benefits (low fees, diversified exposure) and ideal use cases (core holdings, cost-effective diversification).

### 5.13 Bonds & Fixed Income Approaches
This subsection covers different types of bonds (government vs. corporate), bond laddering strategies, and the importance of credit ratings in assessing default risk.

### 5.14 Alternative Investments
This subsection explores alternative investments including:
- Commodities: Useful as inflation hedges but volatile in the short term
- Real Estate: Potential for steady rental income and capital appreciation
- Hedge Funds/Private Equity: Less liquid investments with different return profiles and added complexity

### 5.15 Portfolio Performance Metrics
This subsection covers metrics for evaluating portfolio performance:
- Absolute Returns: Overall gains or losses in monetary terms
- Relative Returns: Comparing performance to a benchmark
- Risk-Adjusted Metrics: Sharpe Ratio, Sortino Ratio for evaluating returns in relation to risk

### 5.16 Portfolio Drawdowns & Recovery
This subsection explains the concept of drawdowns (peak-to-trough declines), their importance in risk management, and recovery tactics including adjusting asset allocation and rebalancing into undervalued assets.

### 5.17 Tax Considerations
This subsection covers tax-efficient investing strategies, differences in taxation between dividends and capital gains, and tax-loss harvesting techniques.

### 5.18 Example Allocations for Different Risk Profiles
This subsection provides example portfolio allocations for different risk profiles:
- Conservative: Lower volatility, lower growth potential
- Moderate: Balanced approach
- Aggressive: Higher potential returns with more significant drawdowns

### 5.19 Creating a Personal Investment Policy Statement (IPS)
This subsection explains how to create a written document outlining investment objectives, risk tolerance, target allocation, and rebalancing strategy. It covers key sections of an IPS and why it helps maintain discipline during market turbulence.

### 5.20 Scenario Planning & Stress Testing
This subsection covers how to test portfolios against hypothetical events (market crashes, recessions, inflation) and make adjustments based on identified weaknesses.

### 5.21 Putting It All Together: Sample Portfolio Construction
This subsection provides a case example of portfolio construction for a 35-year-old investor with moderate risk tolerance and a 25-year horizon, demonstrating how to apply the concepts from the chapter.

### 5.22 Conclusion & Next Steps
This subsection recaps key concepts from the chapter, emphasizing that portfolio construction is about balancing risk and reward through deliberate asset allocation, diversification, and rebalancing. It provides action items for readers and looks ahead to the next chapter.
