# 5.21 Putting It All Together: Sample Portfolio Construction

## Introduction
After exploring the various components of portfolio construction throughout this chapter, it's time to synthesize these concepts into practical examples. This subsection demonstrates how to build complete portfolios for different investor profiles, walking through the entire portfolio construction process from initial assessment to implementation and ongoing management. By examining these sample portfolios, you'll gain insights into how the theoretical concepts translate into real-world investment decisions.

## The Portfolio Construction Process: A Comprehensive Framework

### Step 1: Investor Assessment

#### Case Example: The Johnsons
To illustrate the portfolio construction process, let's consider a hypothetical investor profile:

**The Johnsons: Mid-Career Professionals**
- **Ages**: Michael (42) and Sarah (40)
- **Occupation**: Software engineer and healthcare administrator
- **Annual Income**: $210,000 combined
- **Net Worth**: $750,000 (excluding primary residence)
- **Current Investments**: $420,000 (mostly in employer retirement plans)
- **Financial Goals**:
  - Retirement at age 60 (18-20 years)
  - College funding for two children (ages 10 and 12)
  - Home renovation in 3-5 years ($75,000)
- **Risk Profile**: Moderate risk tolerance with long-term perspective
- **Tax Situation**: 24% federal tax bracket, high-tax state

#### Needs Analysis
Based on the Johnsons' profile, we can identify their key investment needs:

**Time Horizons**:
- Long-term (18-20 years) for retirement
- Medium-term (6-10 years) for college funding
- Short-term (3-5 years) for home renovation

**Required Returns**:
- Retirement: Approximately 6-7% annualized to meet retirement income goals
- College: 5-6% annualized to supplement education savings
- Home Renovation: 3-4% annualized with capital preservation focus

**Risk Considerations**:
- Moderate overall risk tolerance
- Lower risk capacity for short-term goals
- Higher risk capacity for retirement assets
- Need to balance growth with increasing stability as retirement approaches

**Tax Efficiency Needs**:
- Maximize tax-advantaged accounts
- Strategic asset location
- Tax-efficient investment selection for taxable accounts

### Step 2: Asset Allocation Design

#### Strategic Asset Allocation
Based on the Johnsons' profile, we can develop a strategic asset allocation that aligns with their goals, time horizons, and risk tolerance:

**Overall Portfolio Asset Allocation**:
- **Equities**: 60%
- **Fixed Income**: 30%
- **Alternatives**: 5%
- **Cash/Cash Equivalents**: 5%

**Goal-Specific Allocations**:

**Retirement Portfolio (70% of total assets)**:
- **Equities**: 70%

  - U.S. Mid Cap: 10%
  - U.S. Small Cap: 5%
  - International Developed: 15%
  - Emerging Markets: 10%
- **Fixed Income**: 20%
  - U.S. Aggregate Bonds: 10%
  - Corporate Bonds: 5%
  - International Bonds: 5%
- **Alternatives**: 10%
  - REITs: 5%
  - Commodities: 3%
  - Infrastructure: 2%
- **Cash**: 0%

**College Funding Portfolio (20% of total assets)**:
- **Equities**: 50%
  - U.S. Large Cap: 25%
  - U.S. Mid Cap: 10%
  - International Developed: 15%
- **Fixed Income**: 45%
  - Short/Intermediate-Term Bonds: 30%
  - TIPS: 15%
- **Alternatives**: 0%
- **Cash**: 5%

**Home Renovation Portfolio (10% of total assets)**:
- **Equities**: 20%
  - Dividend-focused equities: 20%
- **Fixed Income**: 70%
  - Short-Term Bonds: 50%
  - Ultra-Short Bonds: 20%

#### Allocation Rationale
The allocation strategy addresses the Johnsons' needs in several ways:

**Retirement Portfolio**:
- Growth-oriented with 70% equity allocation appropriate for 18-20 year horizon
- Significant international exposure (25%) for diversification
- Limited bond allocation focused on total return rather than income
- Alternative investments for additional diversification and inflation protection

**College Funding Portfolio**:
- Moderate growth with capital preservation becoming increasingly important
- More conservative than retirement portfolio given shorter time horizon
- TIPS component for inflation protection (important for education costs)
- Small cash position for near-term expenses

**Home Renovation Portfolio**:
- Capital preservation focus with limited growth component
- High allocation to short-term fixed income for stability
- Small equity component for modest growth potential
- Cash reserve for immediate access if needed sooner than planned

### Step 3: Investment Selection

#### Investment Vehicle Selection
For each asset class, appropriate investment vehicles can be selected based on the Johnsons' circumstances:

**Equity Investments**:

**U.S. Large Cap**:
- Core: S&P 500 Index ETF (e.g., VOO, IVV, or SPY)
- Complement: Quality factor ETF (e.g., QUAL) for downside protection
- Rationale: Low-cost core exposure with factor tilt for risk management

**U.S. Mid Cap**:
- Mid Cap Index ETF (e.g., VO or IJH)
- Rationale: Broad exposure to mid-sized companies with growth potential

**U.S. Small Cap**:
- Small Cap Value ETF (e.g., VBR or IJS)
- Rationale: Value tilt to mitigate small cap volatility

**International Developed**:
- Developed Markets ETF (e.g., VEA or IEFA)
- International Dividend ETF (e.g., IDV)
- Rationale: Broad international exposure with income component

**Emerging Markets**:
- Emerging Markets ETF (e.g., VWO or IEMG)
- Rationale: Growth potential with acceptable volatility given time horizon

**Fixed Income Investments**:

**U.S. Aggregate Bonds**:
- Total Bond Market ETF (e.g., BND or AGG)
- Rationale: Broad fixed income exposure with moderate duration

**Corporate Bonds**:
- Investment Grade Corporate Bond ETF (e.g., LQD)
- Rationale: Yield enhancement with acceptable credit risk

**International Bonds**:
- International Bond ETF, Currency Hedged (e.g., BNDX)
- Rationale: Diversification without currency risk

**Short/Intermediate-Term Bonds**:
- Short-Term Bond ETF (e.g., BSV or VCSH)
- Intermediate-Term Bond ETF (e.g., IGIB)
- Rationale: Lower duration risk for shorter time horizons

**TIPS**:
- TIPS ETF (e.g., VTIP or SCHP)
- Rationale: Inflation protection for education expenses

**Ultra-Short Bonds**:
- Ultra-Short Bond ETF (e.g., ICSH or GSY)
- Rationale: Minimal interest rate risk for near-term goals

**Alternative Investments**:

**REITs**:
- REIT ETF (e.g., VNQ or SCHH)
- Rationale: Real estate exposure for diversification and inflation protection

**Commodities**:
- Broad-Based Commodities ETF (e.g., PDBC or COMT)
- Rationale: Inflation hedge with low correlation to traditional assets

**Infrastructure**:
- Global Infrastructure ETF (e.g., IGF)
- Rationale: Stable cash flows with inflation-linked revenues

**Cash/Cash Equivalents**:
- High-Yield Savings Account
- Money Market Fund (e.g., VMFXX)
- Rationale: Capital preservation with some yield

#### Investment Selection Criteria
The investment selections above were made based on several criteria:

**Cost Efficiency**:
- Low expense ratios (generally under 0.20% for core positions)
- Minimal trading costs through commission-free ETFs
- Tax efficiency for taxable accounts

**Diversification**:
- Broad market coverage within each asset class
- Complementary exposures across the portfolio
- Factor diversification where appropriate

**Implementation Efficiency**:
- Liquid investment vehicles
- Ease of rebalancing
- Availability across different account types

**Risk Management**:
- Quality and value tilts for downside protection
- Duration management for interest rate risk
- Currency hedging for international fixed income

### Step 4: Portfolio Implementation

#### Account Location Strategy
Optimizing which investments go into which accounts based on tax efficiency:

**Tax-Advantaged Accounts (401(k)s, IRAs)**:
- REITs (high dividend income, non-qualified)
- Taxable bonds (interest taxed as ordinary income)
- Actively managed funds (if used)
- Commodities ETFs (potentially complex tax treatment)
- High-turnover equity strategies

**Taxable Accounts**:
- Broad market equity index ETFs (tax-efficient)
- Municipal bonds (tax-exempt interest)
- International equity ETFs (foreign tax credit potential)
- Tax-managed funds
- Individual stocks for tax-loss harvesting opportunities

**529 College Savings Plans**:
- Age-based options aligned with children's time horizons
- Or custom allocation using:
  - Stock index funds for longer horizons
  - Bond funds as college approaches
  - Cash/stable value for imminent expenses

#### Implementation Plan
A phased approach to implementing the portfolio:

**Phase 1: Account Consolidation and Organization**:
- Consolidate old 401(k)s into IRAs where appropriate
- Establish dedicated accounts for each major goal
- Set up automatic contributions to all accounts

**Phase 2: Core Position Implementation**:
- Implement core positions in each asset class
- Focus on largest allocations first
- Establish proper asset location

**Phase 3: Complementary Position Implementation**:
- Add factor tilts and complementary positions
- Implement alternative investments
- Fine-tune fixed income allocations

**Phase 4: Ongoing Management Setup**:
- Establish rebalancing schedule and thresholds
- Set up systematic investment plan
- Create monitoring dashboard

### Step 5: Ongoing Management

#### Rebalancing Strategy
A systematic approach to maintaining the target allocation:

**Threshold Rebalancing**:
- Relative Thresholds: Rebalance when any asset class deviates by more than 5% from its target
- Absolute Thresholds: Rebalance when equity allocation exceeds 65% or falls below 55%

**Implementation Methods**:
- Use new contributions to address allocation drift
- Rebalance within tax-advantaged accounts first
- Consider tax implications for taxable account rebalancing
- Utilize tax-loss harvesting opportunities when rebalancing taxable accounts

#### Monitoring Framework
Regular review process to ensure the portfolio remains on track:

**Quarterly Reviews**:
- Check asset allocation against targets
- Review performance relative to benchmarks
- Assess progress toward financial goals
- Identify rebalancing needs

**Annual Comprehensive Review**:
- Reassess risk tolerance and capacity
- Update financial goals and time horizons
- Review investment selection
- Tax planning and harvesting opportunities
- Consider fee optimization opportunities

**Trigger-Based Reviews**:
- Major market movements (±10%)
- Significant life events
- Changes in tax laws
- New investment options becoming available

## Common Portfolio Construction Mistakes and Solutions

### Mistake 1: Excessive Complexity
- **Problem**: Too many holdings, overlapping exposures, difficult to monitor
- **Solution**: Simplify with core positions, limit number of holdings, focus on material allocations

### Mistake 2: Recency Bias in Allocation
- **Problem**: Overweighting recent top performers, chasing returns
- **Solution**: Maintain discipline with strategic allocation, rebalance systematically

### Mistake 3: Inadequate Risk Assessment
- **Problem**: Misalignment between actual risk tolerance and portfolio risk
- **Solution**: Honest risk tolerance assessment, stress testing, scenario analysis

### Mistake 4: Neglecting Investment Costs
- **Problem**: High expense ratios, trading costs, and taxes eroding returns
- **Solution**: Focus on low-cost implementation, tax-efficient placement, minimize turnover

### Mistake 5: Insufficient Diversification
- **Problem**: Concentration in familiar assets or sectors
- **Solution**: Ensure true diversification across and within asset classes

## Key Takeaways
- Portfolio construction is a systematic process that begins with understanding investor circumstances and goals
- Strategic asset allocation should align with time horizons, risk tolerance, and required returns
- Investment selection should consider cost, diversification, implementation efficiency, and risk management
- Account location strategies can significantly enhance after-tax returns
- Ongoing management through systematic rebalancing and regular reviews is essential for long-term success
- Different investor profiles require tailored portfolio solutions
- Specialized approaches like ESG, income-focused, or low-maintenance portfolios require specific considerations
- Common mistakes include excessive complexity, recency bias, inadequate risk assessment, high costs, and insufficient diversification
- The most successful portfolios balance theoretical principles with practical implementation considerations
