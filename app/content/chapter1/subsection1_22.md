# 1.22 Global Investing

## Introduction
In today's interconnected world, limiting investments to your home country means missing out on significant opportunities. Global investing—allocating capital to markets and companies outside your domestic borders—provides access to growth opportunities, diversification benefits, and exposure to different economic cycles. This section explores the fundamentals of international investing, including the differences between domestic and international markets, emerging versus developed economies, currency considerations, diversification benefits, and approaches to managing geopolitical risks.

## International vs. Domestic Markets

### The Global Investment Landscape
The global investment universe is vast and diverse, offering opportunities far beyond any single domestic market.

**Global Market Capitalization Distribution**
As of 2023, the approximate distribution of global equity market capitalization:
- United States: ~60%
- Europe: ~15%
- Japan: ~6%
- China: ~5%
- United Kingdom: ~4%
- Canada: ~3%
- Other markets: ~7%

This distribution changes over time as economies grow at different rates and new markets emerge.

**Home Country Bias**
Most investors significantly overweight their home country in their portfolios:
- Average U.S. investor: 70-80% in U.S. stocks (vs. ~60% of global market cap)
- Average Japanese investor: 60-70% in Japanese stocks (vs. ~6% of global market cap)
- Average UK investor: 50-60% in UK stocks (vs. ~4% of global market cap)

This home bias limits diversification and potential returns.

### Comparing International and Domestic Investing

**Potential Advantages of International Investing**
- **Expanded Opportunity Set**: Access to thousands of additional companies
- **Growth Potential**: Exposure to faster-growing economies
- **Diversification**: Different economic and market cycles
- **Sector Exposure**: Access to industries underrepresented in domestic markets
- **Currency Diversification**: Potential hedge against domestic currency weakness
- **Valuation Opportunities**: Markets trading at different valuation multiples

**Potential Challenges of International Investing**
- **Currency Risk**: Exchange rate fluctuations can impact returns
- **Political and Regulatory Risk**: Different legal systems and political stability
- **Information Asymmetry**: Less familiar companies and markets
- **Higher Costs**: Often higher expense ratios and transaction costs
- **Tax Complexity**: Foreign tax withholding and reporting requirements
- **Market Structure Differences**: Trading hours, settlement procedures, etc.

**Performance Patterns**
International and domestic markets tend to alternate leadership over multi-year cycles:

**U.S. vs. International Market Leadership Cycles**
- 1970s: International outperformed
- 1980s: Mixed performance
- 1990s: U.S. outperformed
- 2000-2007: International outperformed
- 2008-2023: U.S. outperformed

These cycles can last 5-10 years, making diversification across regions important for long-term investors.

### International Investment Vehicles

**Direct International Investing**
- **Individual Foreign Stocks**: Direct ownership of foreign company shares
  - Advantages: Precise control, potentially lower ongoing costs
  - Disadvantages: Higher transaction costs, research challenges, tax complexity

- **American Depositary Receipts (ADRs)**: U.S.-traded certificates representing foreign shares
  - Advantages: Trade on U.S. exchanges in USD, familiar structure
  - Disadvantages: Limited selection, potential premium/discount to underlying shares

**Pooled International Investments**
- **International Mutual Funds**: Professionally managed portfolios of foreign securities
  - Advantages: Professional management, diversification, simplified taxes
  - Disadvantages: Higher expense ratios, potential capital gains distributions

- **International ETFs**: Exchange-traded funds tracking international indexes
  - Advantages: Lower costs than mutual funds, intraday trading, tax efficiency
  - Disadvantages: May not fully replicate index, tracking error

- **Global/International Index Funds**: Passively managed funds tracking international indexes
  - Advantages: Low costs, broad diversification, transparent holdings
  - Disadvantages: Limited to index constituents, market-cap weighting biases

**Specialized International Investments**
- **Country-Specific Funds**: Focus on a single country (e.g., Japan, China, Brazil)
  - Advantages: Targeted exposure to specific markets
  - Disadvantages: Concentrated risk, higher volatility

- **Regional Funds**: Focus on specific regions (e.g., Europe, Asia, Latin America)
  - Advantages: Broader than country funds, regional economic exposure
  - Disadvantages: Still more concentrated than global funds

- **International Sector Funds**: Focus on specific sectors globally
  - Advantages: Global exposure to specific industries
  - Disadvantages: Sector concentration risk

### International Allocation Strategies

**Market Capitalization Weighting**
- Allocate according to global market capitalization
- Currently implies ~60% U.S., ~40% international
- Advantages: Objective, reflects global investment opportunity set
- Disadvantages: Can lead to concentration in largest markets

**Equal Weighting Approach**
- Equal allocation to major regions or countries
- Example: 25% each to U.S., Europe, Asia-Pacific, Emerging Markets
- Advantages: Prevents domination by any single region
- Disadvantages: May overweight smaller, less liquid markets

**GDP Weighting**
- Allocate based on each country's/region's economic output
- Currently implies higher allocation to emerging markets than market cap weighting
- Advantages: Reflects economic activity rather than market valuation
- Disadvantages: Doesn't account for investability or corporate governance

**Strategic Allocation Ranges**
Common international allocation recommendations for U.S.-based investors:
- Conservative: 20-30% international
- Moderate: 30-40% international
- Aggressive: 40-50% international

These ranges provide meaningful diversification while acknowledging home country preferences.

## Emerging vs. Developed Markets

### Defining Market Classifications
Investment markets are typically classified as either developed, emerging, or frontier based on economic development, market accessibility, and infrastructure.

**Developed Markets**
Characteristics:
- Advanced economies with high per capita income
- Well-established regulatory and legal frameworks
- Highly liquid capital markets with strong investor protections
- Sophisticated market infrastructure
- Political stability and transparent governance
- Lower growth but generally lower volatility

Examples: United States, Canada, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Japan, Australia

**Emerging Markets**
Characteristics:
- Middle-income economies in development transition
- Evolving regulatory and legal frameworks
- Improving but still developing capital markets
- Growing middle class and consumer base
- Higher economic growth potential
- Higher volatility and political risk
- Less transparent corporate governance

Examples: China, India, Brazil, Russia, South Korea, Taiwan, South Africa, Mexico

**Frontier Markets**
Characteristics:
- Lower-income economies at early development stages
- Limited market accessibility and liquidity
- Minimal regulatory infrastructure
- Higher political and operational risks
- Potentially highest growth potential
- Highest volatility and uncertainty
- Limited correlation with global markets

Examples: Vietnam, Nigeria, Kenya, Morocco, Romania, Kuwait, Bangladesh

### Emerging Markets Investment Case

**Growth Potential**
- Faster GDP growth than developed markets (typically 2-4% higher annually)
- Expanding middle class (estimated 3 billion new middle-class consumers by 2030)
- Demographic advantages (younger populations in many emerging countries)
- Infrastructure development needs
- Technology leapfrogging (e.g., mobile banking adoption)

**Diversification Benefits**
- Different economic cycles than developed markets
- Varying commodity dependencies and export profiles
- Increasing but still moderate correlation with developed markets
- Different sector weightings than developed market indexes

**Valuation Opportunities**
- Often trade at lower P/E ratios than developed markets
- Higher dividend yields in many emerging markets
- Potential for multiple expansion as markets mature
- Inefficiencies creating active management opportunities

### Emerging Markets Risks and Challenges

**Political and Governance Risks**
- Government instability and policy changes
- Nationalization or expropriation risks
- Corruption and weak rule of law
- Capital controls and repatriation restrictions
- Sanctions and international relations issues

**Market Structure Issues**
- Limited liquidity in smaller markets
- Foreign ownership restrictions
- Less transparent corporate governance
- Concentrated ownership structures
- Limited shareholder rights

**Economic Vulnerabilities**
- Currency volatility and devaluation risk
- Inflation sensitivity
- External debt dependencies
- Commodity price sensitivity
- Banking system fragility

**Practical Investment Challenges**
- Higher transaction costs and taxes
- Limited research coverage
- Accounting standard differences
- Time zone and language barriers
- Higher investment minimums for direct access

### Balancing Developed and Emerging Market Exposure

**Allocation Considerations**
- Emerging markets represent ~12% of global market capitalization but ~40% of global GDP
- Common allocation ranges for emerging markets within international exposure:
  - Conservative: 10-20% of international allocation (3-8% of total portfolio)
  - Moderate: 20-30% of international allocation (6-12% of total portfolio)
  - Aggressive: 30-40% of international allocation (12-20% of total portfolio)

**Implementation Approaches**
- **Core-Satellite**: Broad emerging markets fund as core with country-specific satellites
- **Active-Passive Blend**: Passive for developed markets, active for emerging markets
- **Factor Tilts**: Quality and minimum volatility factors can reduce emerging market risks
- **Phased Approach**: Gradually increase emerging market exposure over time

**Rebalancing Discipline**
- Emerging markets often experience larger swings than developed markets
- Regular rebalancing helps maintain target allocations
- Can provide natural "buy low, sell high" discipline
- Consider wider bands for emerging markets (e.g., ±5% vs. ±3% for developed)

## Currency Considerations

### Understanding Currency Risk
When investing internationally, returns are affected by both the performance of the underlying investments and changes in exchange rates.

**Components of International Returns**
Total Return = Local Market Return + Currency Return + Interaction Effect

Example:
- Japanese stock returns 10% in yen
- Yen strengthens 5% against USD
- Total return for U.S. investor ≈ 15.5% (10% + 5% + 0.5%)

Conversely, if the yen weakened 5%, total return would be approximately 4.5%.

**Currency Impact Over Time**
- Short-term: Currency movements can dominate returns
- Medium-term: Currency effects often significant but secondary to market returns
- Long-term: Currency effects tend to mean-revert and contribute less to total returns

**Currency Correlation Effects**
- Developed market currencies often move counter to their equity markets during crises
- Emerging market currencies typically move in the same direction as their equity markets
- These correlation patterns affect the diversification benefits of international investing

### Currency Hedging Strategies

**Unhedged Approach**
- Accept full currency exposure
- No hedging costs or complexity
- Full diversification benefit against home currency
- Higher short-term volatility

**Fully Hedged Approach**
- Eliminate currency exposure through forward contracts or futures
- Reduces overall volatility (typically by 2-4% annually for developed markets)
- Incurs hedging costs (0.1-0.5% annually for developed markets, higher for emerging)
- Removes currency diversification benefit

**Partial Hedging**
- Hedge a portion of currency exposure (e.g., 50%)
- Balance between volatility reduction and diversification
- Reduces hedging costs compared to full hedge
- Simplifies decision-making

**Strategic Hedging**
- Vary hedge ratio based on currency valuations or trends
- Hedge overvalued currencies, leave undervalued unhedged
- Requires currency valuation model or methodology
- More complex to implement and monitor

### Currency Hedging by Market Type

**Developed Markets**
- Lower hedging costs due to liquid currency markets
- Many hedged ETF and fund options available
- Hedging more effective at reducing volatility
- Common approach: 50-100% hedge ratio

**Emerging Markets**
- Higher hedging costs due to less liquid currency markets
- Fewer hedged fund options available
- Hedging less effective due to higher equity volatility
- Common approach: 0-50% hedge ratio or unhedged

**Practical Implementation**
- **Fund Selection**: Choose between hedged and unhedged funds
- **Currency Overlay**: Add separate currency hedges to unhedged positions
- **Hedged ETFs**: Use currency-hedged ETFs for simple implementation
- **Dynamic Products**: Some funds adjust hedge ratios based on market conditions

### Currency Diversification Benefits
Beyond risk management, currencies can provide portfolio diversification:

**Safe Haven Currencies**
- U.S. Dollar (USD): Traditional safe haven during global crises
- Japanese Yen (JPY): Often strengthens during market stress
- Swiss Franc (CHF): Historical stability and neutrality

**Commodity Currencies**
- Canadian Dollar (CAD): Linked to oil and natural resources
- Australian Dollar (AUD): Tied to mining and agricultural exports
- Norwegian Krone (NOK): Connected to oil exports

**Emerging Market Currencies**
- Higher yields but greater volatility
- Potential for long-term appreciation as economies develop
- Often move with risk sentiment (weaken during global risk aversion)

**Strategic Currency Positioning**
- Consider your home currency's characteristics
- Diversify across currency types for balanced exposure
- Align currency exposure with macroeconomic views when appropriate
- Remember that currency speculation is different from strategic diversification

## Global Diversification Benefits

### The Mathematics of Diversification
Global diversification works because different markets don't move in perfect unison.

**Correlation Concepts**
- Correlation measures how closely two assets move together (scale of -1 to +1)
- Perfect positive correlation (+1): Assets move identically
- No correlation (0): Assets move independently
- Perfect negative correlation (-1): Assets move in opposite directions
- Lower correlations between assets improve diversification benefits

**International Equity Correlations**
Approximate long-term correlations between major markets:
- U.S. to Developed International: 0.80-0.85
- U.S. to Emerging Markets: 0.70-0.75
- Developed International to Emerging Markets: 0.75-0.80
- Individual countries to each other: 0.40-0.70

While correlations have increased over time, they remain below 1.0, providing diversification benefits.

### Risk Reduction Through Global Diversification

**Volatility Reduction**
Adding international investments to a domestic portfolio can reduce overall volatility:

**Example: Portfolio Volatility Comparison**
- 100% U.S. Stocks: 15% annual volatility
- 70% U.S./30% International: 14% annual volatility
- 60% U.S./40% International: 13.5% annual volatility

The exact reduction depends on the time period and specific allocations.

**Drawdown Mitigation**
Different markets experience peak drawdowns at different times:

**Historical Maximum Drawdowns (2000-2023)**
- U.S. Market: -55% (2007-2009)
- European Markets: -60% (2007-2009)
- Japanese Market: -56% (2007-2009)
- Emerging Markets: -62% (2007-2009)

While the 2008 financial crisis affected all markets, other corrections have been more regional:
- 2000-2002 Tech Bubble: Primarily U.S. and developed markets
- 2011 European Debt Crisis: Primarily European markets
- 2015 China Slowdown: Primarily emerging markets

**Sequence of Returns Risk Reduction**
For retirees, global diversification can reduce sequence risk:
- Different markets peak and trough at different times
- Withdrawals can be targeted from better-performing regions
- Reduces the impact of extended domestic market underperformance

### Return Enhancement Opportunities

**Capturing Global Growth**
- Access to faster-growing economies and companies
- Exposure to industries underrepresented in domestic market
- Participation in structural growth trends (e.g., emerging market consumers)

**Valuation Disparities**
- Markets trade at different valuations (P/E ratios, dividend yields)
- Opportunity to invest in undervalued regions
- Rebalancing across regions captures "buy low, sell high" benefits

**Sector Diversification**
Different markets have different sector compositions:
- U.S.: Technology and consumer services dominant
- Europe: Financials, industrials, and consumer staples prominent
- Japan: Industrials and consumer discretionary emphasized
- Emerging Markets: Financials, technology, and materials significant

This sector diversification provides exposure to different economic drivers.

### The Efficient Frontier Effect
Modern Portfolio Theory demonstrates that combining assets with less than perfect correlation improves the risk-return profile:

```
Expected
Return
^
|                   * Global Portfolio
|              *
|         *
|    *
| *
|    * Domestic Portfolio
|
+--------------------------------> Risk (Volatility)
```

A globally diversified portfolio can achieve:
- Higher returns for the same level of risk, or
- Lower risk for the same level of return

### Evolving Correlations and Globalization
The benefits of international diversification have changed over time:

**Increasing Correlations**
- Global correlations have generally increased over the past 30 years
- Globalization has connected economies and markets more closely
- Technology enables instantaneous information transmission globally
- Multinational corporations operate across borders

**Correlation During Crises**
- Correlations tend to spike during global market stress
- Diversification benefits can diminish when most needed
- However, recovery patterns often differ across regions
- Correlations typically decrease during normal market conditions

**Implications for Investors**
- Benefits of international diversification remain significant but reduced
- More international exposure may be needed to achieve the same diversification benefit
- Regional and country selection becomes more important
- Alternative assets may provide additional diversification

## Geopolitical Risks

### Types of Geopolitical Risks
International investors face various geopolitical risks that can impact investments:

**Political Instability**
- Government changes and policy shifts
- Civil unrest and protests
- Coups and regime changes
- Constitutional crises

**International Conflicts**
- Wars and military actions
- Trade disputes and sanctions
- Diplomatic tensions
- Territorial disputes

**Regulatory and Legal Risks**
- Nationalization or expropriation
- Capital controls and investment restrictions
- Changing regulatory environments
- Legal system differences and enforcement issues

**Economic Policy Risks**
- Currency devaluations
- Debt defaults
- Taxation changes
- Monetary policy divergence

### Assessing Geopolitical Risk

**Country Risk Assessment Frameworks**
- Political stability indicators
- Rule of law and property rights protections
- Economic freedom measures
- Corruption perception indexes
- Ease of doing business rankings

**Information Sources**
- Economist Intelligence Unit
- World Bank Governance Indicators
- Transparency International
- Heritage Foundation Economic Freedom Index
- Country risk ratings from rating agencies

**Risk Premium Approach**
- Higher required returns for higher-risk countries
- Equity risk premium adjustments
- Sovereign bond spread analysis
- Implied volatility measures

**Scenario Analysis**
- Develop multiple potential geopolitical scenarios
- Assess probability and potential impact of each
- Stress test portfolio against adverse scenarios
- Identify vulnerabilities and potential responses

### Managing Geopolitical Risk

**Diversification Strategies**
- Spread investments across multiple countries and regions
- Limit single-country exposure (typically 5-10% maximum)
- Balance developed and emerging market allocations
- Consider political correlations (not just economic)

**Investment Vehicle Selection**
- Actively managed funds with geopolitical expertise
- ETFs for quick tactical adjustments
- ADRs vs. local shares (different risk profiles)
- Derivatives for hedging specific risks

**Position Sizing and Limits**
- Smaller positions in higher-risk countries
- Gradual position building in new markets
- Clear exit thresholds for deteriorating situations
- Regular review of country exposures

**Monitoring Systems**
- Regular review of geopolitical developments
- News alerts for significant events
- Country risk dashboard
- Periodic reassessment of risk exposures

### Responding to Geopolitical Events

**Reactive vs. Proactive Approaches**
- Reactive: Respond after events occur
- Proactive: Position in anticipation of potential events
- Hybrid: Scenario planning with predefined response triggers

**Common Mistakes in Geopolitical Risk Management**
- Overreacting to headlines and news flow
- Confusing short-term noise with long-term structural changes
- Assuming past geopolitical patterns will repeat exactly
- Failing to distinguish between risks and uncertainties

**Historical Perspective on Geopolitical Shocks**
- Markets typically overreact initially to geopolitical events
- Most geopolitical shocks have relatively short market impact (3-6 months)
- Fundamental economic factors reassert dominance over longer periods
- Some events create lasting structural changes requiring portfolio adjustment

**Opportunity in Crisis**
- Geopolitical crises often create valuation opportunities
- Indiscriminate selling can affect strong and weak companies alike
- Local market participants may have better information than foreign investors
- Patient capital can benefit from short-term volatility

## Practical Global Investing Approaches

### Building a Global Portfolio

**Core-Satellite Approach**
- **Core**: Broad global or international index funds (60-80% of international allocation)
- **Satellites**: Specific countries, regions, or themes (20-40% of international allocation)
- Advantages: Simplicity, low cost core, targeted exposures in satellites
- Example: Core global ex-US index fund + emerging markets fund + Japan fund

**Factor-Based Global Approach**
- Apply factor strategies (value, quality, momentum) across global markets
- Can help manage risks in higher-risk markets
- Potentially enhance returns through systematic factor exposures
- Example: Global value ETF + global quality ETF + global minimum volatility ETF

**Active-Passive Blend**
- Passive for efficient markets (U.S., developed markets)
- Active for less efficient markets (emerging, frontier markets)
- Balances cost efficiency with alpha potential
- Example: Developed markets index fund + active emerging markets fund

**Thematic Global Investing**
- Focus on global themes rather than countries or regions
- Examples: Clean energy, cybersecurity, aging population, water resources
- Provides exposure across geographies united by common theme
- Can complement traditional geographic diversification

### Implementation Vehicles

**Fund Selection Criteria**
- Expense ratio and total cost (trading costs, tax efficiency)
- Tracking error (for passive vehicles)
- Historical performance and risk metrics
- Fund size and liquidity
- Manager experience and process (for active funds)
- Currency hedging approach

**ETFs vs. Mutual Funds for International Exposure**
- ETFs: Lower costs, intraday trading, potentially more tax-efficient
- Mutual Funds: Potentially better for less liquid markets, no bid-ask spreads
- Both: Available in active and passive versions, broad range of exposures

**Direct Investment Considerations**
- Higher complexity and costs
- Greater control over specific exposures
- Tax implications (foreign tax credits, reporting requirements)
- Custody and trading logistics

**Robo-Advisors and Model Portfolios**
- Many offer global diversification as core feature
- Automated rebalancing across regions
- Often use low-cost ETFs for implementation
- Simplifies international allocation decisions

### Practical Allocation Examples

**Conservative Global Allocation**
- 70% Domestic / 30% International
- International breakdown:
  - 80% Developed Markets (24% of total)
  - 20% Emerging Markets (6% of total)
- Implementation: 2-3 broad funds
- Currency: Primarily unhedged

**Moderate Global Allocation**
- 60% Domestic / 40% International
- International breakdown:
  - 70% Developed Markets (28% of total)
  - 30% Emerging Markets (12% of total)
- Implementation: 3-5 funds with some regional tilts
- Currency: Partial hedging of developed markets

**Aggressive Global Allocation**
- 50% Domestic / 50% International
- International breakdown:
  - 60% Developed Markets (30% of total)
  - 30% Emerging Markets (15% of total)
  - 10% Frontier Markets (5% of total)
- Implementation: 5-8 funds with specific country and thematic exposures
- Currency: Strategic hedging based on currency outlook

### Ongoing Management of Global Portfolios

**Rebalancing Considerations**
- International markets can drift significantly from targets
- Consider wider rebalancing bands for international (±5% vs. ±3% domestic)
- Tax-efficient rebalancing through new contributions
- Potential for tactical rebalancing after significant divergences

**Tax Management**
- Foreign tax credits for withholding taxes
- Asset location (international funds in tax-advantaged accounts when possible)
- Tax-loss harvesting opportunities across regions
- PFIC (Passive Foreign Investment Company) considerations for direct investments

**Monitoring and Evaluation**
- Regular review of regional performance and exposures
- Benchmark comparison (appropriate global indexes)
- Risk metrics across regions (volatility, drawdowns, Sharpe ratio)
- Currency impact assessment

**Staying Informed**
- Global economic indicators and trends
- Political developments in key markets
- Currency movements and central bank policies
- Valuation changes across regions

## Key Takeaways
- Global investing expands your opportunity set beyond domestic markets
- International and domestic markets tend to alternate leadership over multi-year cycles
- Emerging markets offer higher growth potential but with increased volatility and risk
- Currency movements can significantly impact international returns in the short term
- Global diversification can improve risk-adjusted returns through imperfect correlations
- Geopolitical risks require assessment and management through diversification and monitoring
- A structured approach to global allocation helps balance opportunity and risk
- Regular rebalancing across regions maintains target exposures and can enhance returns
