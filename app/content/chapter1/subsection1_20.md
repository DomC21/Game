# 1.20 Common Investing Mistakes

## Introduction
Even the most successful investors make mistakes. What separates good investors from poor ones is not the absence of errors but the ability to recognize, learn from, and avoid repeating them. This section explores the most common investing mistakes that can derail your financial goals and provides practical strategies to avoid them. By understanding these pitfalls, you can improve your investment outcomes and build wealth more effectively over time.

## Timing the Market

### The Allure of Market Timing
Market timing—attempting to predict when markets will rise or fall and adjusting investments accordingly—is one of the most tempting yet dangerous investing strategies.

**Why Investors Try to Time the Market:**
- Fear of losing money in market downturns
- Desire to maximize returns by buying low and selling high
- Media coverage that emphasizes short-term movements
- Overconfidence in ability to predict market movements
- Success stories of those who "got it right" (survivorship bias)

**The Reality of Market Timing:**
- Consistently successful market timing is virtually impossible
- Requires predicting both when to exit and when to re-enter
- Missing just a few of the best market days can dramatically reduce returns
- Transaction costs and taxes further reduce returns from frequent trading
- Even professional managers rarely succeed at market timing consistently

### The Cost of Missing the Best Days
Research consistently shows that missing just a few of the market's best days can significantly impact long-term returns.

**Example: S&P 500 Returns (1999-2019)**
- Fully invested for entire period: 6.1% annualized return
- Missed 10 best days: 2.4% annualized return (60% less)
- Missed 20 best days: -0.1% annualized return (negative)
- Missed 30 best days: -2.4% annualized return (significant loss)

**Why Missing the Best Days Is So Costly:**
- Market gains often come in short, unpredictable bursts
- Many of the best days occur during volatile periods when investors are most likely to be on the sidelines
- About 70% of the market's best days occur within two weeks of its worst days
- Compound growth magnifies the impact of missing strong returns

**Typical Emotional Cycle:**

2. Early recovery occurs with skepticism (hope)

5. Initial declines are dismissed (anxiety, denial)
6. As losses mount, fear takes over (fear, desperation)
7. At market bottoms, investors capitulate (panic, depression)
8. Cycle repeats

This emotional cycle leads investors to buy high (during euphoria) and sell low (during panic)—the opposite of successful investing.

### Alternatives to Market Timing

**Dollar-Cost Averaging**
- Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals
- Automatically buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high
- Removes emotion from the investment process
- Works best with a long time horizon

**Strategic Asset Allocation**
- Determine an appropriate mix of assets based on goals and risk tolerance
- Maintain this allocation through market cycles
- Rebalance periodically to maintain target allocations
- Provides discipline and reduces emotional decision-making

**Tactical Asset Allocation**
- Make modest adjustments to allocations based on valuation metrics
- Use objective indicators rather than predictions or feelings
- Limit adjustments to a predetermined range (e.g., ±5% from targets)
- Maintain core strategic allocation throughout

**Focus on Time in the Market, Not Timing the Market**
- Commit to long-term investment horizon
- Accept that volatility is the price of admission for higher returns
- Develop the emotional discipline to stay invested during downturns
- Remember that time smooths out short-term volatility

## Chasing Performance

### The Performance-Chasing Trap
Performance chasing—investing in whatever has performed well recently—is a common behavior that typically leads to poor results.

**How Performance Chasing Manifests:**
- Buying funds that topped last year's performance charts
- Rotating into the hottest sectors or themes
- Abandoning underperforming investments for recent winners
- Following investment trends highlighted in financial media
- Investing based on short-term (1-3 year) track records

**The Psychology Behind Performance Chasing:**
- Recency bias: Overweighting recent events in decision-making
- FOMO (Fear of Missing Out): Anxiety about missing opportunities
- Extrapolation: Assuming recent trends will continue indefinitely
- Social proof: Following what others are doing
- Availability bias: Focusing on information that's easily recalled

### The Performance Cycle
Investment categories tend to move through cycles of performance, with today's winners often becoming tomorrow's laggards.

**Asset Class Returns by Year (Example)**
```
Year | Top Performer       | Bottom Performer
-----|---------------------|-------------------
2015 | Large Growth Stocks | Commodities
2016 | Small Value Stocks  | Large Growth Stocks
2017 | Emerging Markets    | Commodities
2018 | Cash                | Emerging Markets
2019 | Large Growth Stocks | Cash
2020 | Technology          | Real Estate
2021 | Real Estate         | Bonds
2022 | Energy              | Technology
```

This pattern of rotating leadership makes performance chasing particularly harmful, as investors often buy at or near category peaks and sell at or near bottoms.

### The Cost of Performance Chasing
Research consistently shows that performance-chasing investors earn lower returns than the funds they invest in.

**Morningstar Investor Return Gap Study:**
- Average fund return (2012-2022): 8.3% annually
- Average investor return in those same funds: 6.1% annually
- "Behavior gap": 2.2% annually

This gap occurs because investors tend to buy after periods of strong performance and sell after periods of weak performance, effectively buying high and selling low.

**Impact of Behavior Gap Over Time:**
- $10,000 growing at 8.3% for 30 years: $104,000
- $10,000 growing at 6.1% for 30 years: $58,000
- Difference: $46,000 (44% less wealth)

### Better Approaches to Fund/Investment Selection

**Focus on Process Over Performance**
- Evaluate the investment philosophy and process
- Look for consistency in approach across market cycles
- Consider how returns were achieved, not just their magnitude
- Assess whether past performance aligns with the stated strategy

**Longer Time Horizons**
- Examine performance across full market cycles
- Look at 5-10 year returns rather than 1-3 year returns
- Consider performance in different market environments
- Evaluate risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown)

**Forward-Looking Analysis**
- Current valuations and yield metrics
- Changes in fund management or strategy
- Expense ratios and fee trends
- Fund size relative to its strategy

**Contrarian Thinking**
- Consider currently unpopular but fundamentally sound investments
- Look for areas of persistent undervaluation
- Be willing to be patient with underperforming assets
- Remember that reversion to the mean is a powerful force in markets

## Neglecting Fees and Expenses

### The Hidden Impact of Investment Costs
Investment costs may seem small as percentages, but their impact on long-term returns is substantial due to compounding.

**Types of Investment Costs:**
- **Expense Ratios**: Annual fees charged by funds (typically 0.03%-1.5%)
- **Trading Commissions**: Fees for buying and selling securities
- **Loads**: Sales charges when buying (front-end) or selling (back-end) certain funds
- **Advisory Fees**: Charges for professional investment management (typically 0.25%-1.5% annually)
- **Account Fees**: Maintenance fees for brokerage or retirement accounts
- **Bid-Ask Spreads**: Implicit costs when buying or selling securities
- **Market Impact**: Price movement caused by your own trading

**The Compounding Effect of Fees**
Fees compound negatively over time, creating a growing drag on returns:

**Example: Impact of Different Expense Ratios**
$10,000 invested for 30 years with 7% gross return:
- 0.1% expense ratio: Final value = $75,937 (total fees paid: $3,690)
- 0.5% expense ratio: Final value = $67,676 (total fees paid: $16,951)
- 1.0% expense ratio: Final value = $57,435 (total fees paid: $27,192)
- 2.0% expense ratio: Final value = $42,726 (total fees paid: $41,901)

In the 2% fee scenario, you end up paying more in fees than your initial investment, and your final value is 44% lower than in the 0.1% scenario.

### Fee Awareness and Reduction Strategies

**Know What You're Paying**
- Review fund expense ratios before investing
- Check account statements for fees and commissions
- Request a fee disclosure from financial advisors
- Look for hidden fees in prospectuses and account agreements

**Low-Cost Alternatives**
- Index funds vs. actively managed funds
- ETFs vs. mutual funds (generally)
- Direct indexing vs. packaged products
- Discount brokers vs. full-service brokers

**Fee Comparison Shopping**
- Compare similar funds from different providers
- Evaluate whether premium services justify higher fees
- Consider the all-in cost of your investment approach
- Negotiate fees with advisors when possible

**The Active vs. Passive Decision**
- Active management fees should be justified by value added
- Consider indexing core portfolio positions
- Use active management selectively where it has better odds of success
- Remember that after fees, most active managers underperform their benchmarks

### The "Small" Fees That Matter
Some fees that seem insignificant can have a major impact over time:

**401(k) Administrative Fees**
- Often overlooked but can range from 0.2% to 1.5% annually
- Added on top of underlying fund expenses
- Can reduce retirement savings by 20-30% over a career

**Cash Drag**
- Uninvested cash in funds and accounts
- Typically earns minimal interest while still incurring management fees
- Can reduce returns by 0.1%-0.3% annually

**Tax Inefficiency**
- Not technically a fee but acts like one
- High-turnover strategies generate more taxable distributions
- Can reduce after-tax returns by 1-2% annually in taxable accounts

**Trading Costs Within Funds**
- Not included in expense ratio
- Can add 0.1%-1.0% in additional costs
- Often higher in small-cap, international, and high-turnover funds

## Inadequate Diversification

### Understanding Diversification
Diversification—spreading investments across different assets to reduce risk—is often called the "only free lunch in investing."

**What Diversification Does:**
- Reduces portfolio volatility
- Minimizes the impact of any single investment's poor performance
- Captures returns from multiple market segments
- Provides more consistent performance across market cycles
- Reduces specific company and industry risks

**What Diversification Doesn't Do:**
- Eliminate all risk
- Maximize returns in any single time period
- Prevent losses during broad market declines
- Guarantee positive returns

### Common Diversification Mistakes

**Home Country Bias**
- Overweighting investments in your own country
- Typical U.S. investor holds 70-80% in U.S. stocks (vs. ~60% of global market cap)
- Misses opportunities in faster-growing international markets
- Increases exposure to country-specific risks

**Employer Stock Concentration**
- Overinvesting in your employer's stock
- Creates "double jeopardy" (job and investments tied to same company)
- Historical disasters: Enron, WorldCom, Lehman Brothers
- Recommended maximum: 5-10% of portfolio

**Sector Concentration**
- Overweighting popular or familiar sectors
- Technology concentration in late 1990s
- Financial sector concentration before 2008
- Recent examples: FAANG stocks, cryptocurrency

**False Diversification**
- Owning multiple investments that move similarly
- Example: Owning several large-cap U.S. stock funds with overlapping holdings
- Example: Owning multiple technology stocks thinking they're different
- Correlation matters more than the number of holdings

### Effective Diversification Strategies

**Asset Class Diversification**
- Stocks: Growth potential, inflation protection
- Bonds: Income, stability, deflation protection
- Cash: Liquidity, opportunity reserves
- Alternative assets: Different return patterns

**Geographic Diversification**
- U.S. markets
- Developed international markets
- Emerging markets
- Frontier markets

**Style Diversification**
- Growth vs. value
- Large-cap vs. small-cap
- High-quality vs. distressed
- Momentum vs. mean reversion

**Factor Diversification**
- Market beta
- Size
- Value
- Quality
- Momentum
- Volatility

**Correlation Awareness**
- Focus on how investments perform relative to each other
- Seek assets with low or negative correlations
- Understand that correlations can change in crises
- Regularly review correlation patterns in your portfolio

### The Diversification Sweet Spot
More diversification isn't always better—there's a point of diminishing returns:

```
Risk
Reduction
Benefit
^
|
|    *
|   /
|  /
| /
|/
+-------------------------> Number of Holdings

```

**Empirical Guidelines:**
- Stock portfolios: 15-30 stocks captures ~90% of diversification benefits
- Fund portfolios: 5-10 funds across different categories
- Asset allocation: 3-7 distinct asset classes
- International exposure: Roughly proportional to global market capitalization

## Panic Selling During Downturns

### The Costly Mistake of Panic Selling
Selling investments during market downturns is one of the most damaging investor behaviors.

**Why Investors Panic Sell:**
- Fear of further losses
- Desire to "wait until things settle down"
- Media amplification of negative news
- Social reinforcement (others are selling too)
- Loss of confidence in investment thesis
- Need for emotional relief from anxiety

**The Double Mistake Problem:**
Panic selling requires being right twice:
1. When to sell (usually after markets have already fallen)
2. When to buy back in (usually after markets have already recovered)

Most investors who sell during downturns miss the subsequent recovery, locking in losses and missing out on the rebound.

### Historical Perspective on Market Declines
Market declines are normal and temporary, though they never feel that way in the moment.

**S&P 500 Historical Declines (1928-2023):**
- 5-10% declines: About once per year
- 10-20% corrections: About once every 2-3 years
- 20%+ bear markets: About once every 3-5 years
- 30%+ severe bears: About once every decade
- 50%+ crashes: Three times in past century (1929, 2000, 2008)

**Recovery Patterns:**
- Average bear market decline: 35%
- Average bear market duration: 14 months
- Average time to recovery: 24 months
- Longest recovery period: 5.8 years (1929 crash)
- Shortest recovery period: 4 months (2020 COVID crash)

**Key Insight:** Every single market decline in history has eventually been followed by new all-time highs.

### The Psychological Challenge
Market declines create powerful psychological pressures that make rational decision-making difficult.

**Loss Aversion**
- Losses hurt psychologically about twice as much as gains feel good
- Creates strong desire to stop the pain by selling
- Leads to short-term relief at the expense of long-term returns

**Recency Bias**
- Overweighting recent events in decision-making
- During declines, it feels like markets will keep falling forever
- Difficulty imagining recovery when surrounded by negative news

**Herd Mentality**
- Social reinforcement of selling behavior
- Comfort in doing what others are doing
- Fear of being the "last one out"

**Media Amplification**
- Negative news gets more attention and clicks
- Dramatic coverage of market declines
- Expert predictions of further declines
- Limited historical context provided

### Strategies to Prevent Panic Selling

**Preparation Before Downturns**
- Ensure your asset allocation matches your risk tolerance
- Maintain adequate emergency funds outside the market
- Understand the historical patterns of market volatility
- Write down your investment plan and commitment to stay invested
- Decide in advance how you'll respond to various market scenarios

**During Market Declines**
- Avoid checking your portfolio frequently
- Limit consumption of financial news
- Focus on your long-term goals rather than short-term fluctuations
- Remember that temporary declines are the "cost of admission" for long-term returns
- Consider whether the decline changes your fundamental investment thesis

**Constructive Actions During Downturns**
Instead of selling, consider these alternatives:
- Rebalance to your target asset allocation
- Tax-loss harvest to create tax benefits
- Increase contributions if possible
- Upgrade portfolio quality
- Review and refine your investment plan

**Professional Support**
- Work with a financial advisor who can provide objectivity
- Join investment groups that encourage long-term thinking
- Share your commitment to stay invested with someone who can hold you accountable
- Consider automated investing to remove emotion from the process

### The Opportunity in Market Declines
Market downturns, while emotionally challenging, often present the best investment opportunities.

**Dollar-Cost Averaging Benefit**
- Regular contributions buy more shares when prices are lower
- Automatically takes advantage of market declines
- Removes the pressure of trying to time the bottom

**Valuation Improvement**
- Lower prices mean higher expected future returns
- Dividend yields increase as prices fall
- P/E ratios typically become more attractive

**Historical Perspective on Post-Decline Returns**
Average S&P 500 returns following 20%+ declines:
- 1 year later: +22%
- 3 years later: +41%
- 5 years later: +83%

**Warren Buffett's Wisdom:**
"Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful."

## Other Common Investing Mistakes

### Overtrading
Excessive trading typically reduces returns due to costs and behavioral biases.

**The Problem:**
- Trading costs (commissions, spreads, taxes)
- Increased likelihood of timing errors
- Tendency to sell winners too soon and hold losers too long
- Stress and time commitment

**Research Finding:** Studies show that investors who trade the most earn the lowest returns.

**Solution:**
- Implement a "cooling off" period before making trades
- Maintain a trading journal to track decisions and outcomes
- Set a maximum percentage of portfolio that can be traded annually
- Consider a core-and-satellite approach (stable core with limited trading around the edges)

### Confirmation Bias
Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

**The Problem:**
- Creates blind spots in analysis
- Leads to overconfidence in investment theses
- Prevents recognition of changing fundamentals
- Results in holding losing investments too long

**Solution:**
- Actively seek out opposing viewpoints
- Consider the strongest arguments against your position
- Follow analysts and commentators with different perspectives
- Create a "pre-mortem" (imagine your investment failed and explain why)

### Neglecting Tax Efficiency
Failing to consider tax implications of investment decisions.

**The Problem:**
- High-turnover strategies generating short-term capital gains
- Inefficient asset location (taxable vs. tax-advantaged accounts)
- Missed tax-loss harvesting opportunities
- Unnecessary taxable distributions

**Solution:**
- Hold tax-efficient investments in taxable accounts
- Place tax-inefficient investments in tax-advantaged accounts
- Harvest losses to offset gains
- Consider tax-managed funds and ETFs for taxable accounts
- Be aware of embedded capital gains when buying mutual funds

### Lifestyle Inflation and Inadequate Saving
Investment returns matter less than your savings rate, especially in the early years.

**The Problem:**
- Increasing spending as income rises
- Focusing on investment returns while neglecting savings rate
- Delaying saving until "later"
- Withdrawing retirement funds for non-emergency expenses

**Solution:**
- Automate savings before spending
- Increase savings rate with each income increase
- Set clear savings goals tied to specific life objectives
- Treat retirement accounts as untouchable except in true emergencies

### Recency Bias in Asset Allocation
Overweighting recent performance when making asset allocation decisions.

**The Problem:**
- Increasing allocation to recent winners
- Decreasing allocation to recent underperformers
- Chasing returns rather than maintaining strategic allocation
- Buying high and selling low across asset classes

**Solution:**
- Establish strategic asset allocation based on goals and risk tolerance
- Rebalance regularly to maintain target allocations
- Consider valuation metrics when making tactical adjustments
- Remember that asset class performance is cyclical

## Creating a Mistake-Resistant Investment Approach

### Written Investment Policy Statement
A written plan helps maintain discipline and avoid common mistakes.

**Key Components:**
- Investment goals and time horizons
- Risk tolerance assessment
- Target asset allocation with acceptable ranges
- Criteria for selecting investments
- Rebalancing strategy and triggers
- Conditions under which the plan will be reviewed

**Benefits:**
- Provides clarity during market turbulence
- Creates accountability to your stated strategy
- Reduces emotional decision-making
- Serves as a reference point for evaluating potential changes

### Regular Review Process
Systematic review helps identify and correct mistakes before they become costly.

**Recommended Review Schedule:**
- Monthly: Brief check of account activity (5 minutes)
- Quarterly: Portfolio drift assessment (15 minutes)
- Annually: Comprehensive review of strategy and performance (1-2 hours)
- Major life events: Reassessment of goals and risk tolerance

**Review Questions:**
- Is my asset allocation still aligned with my targets?
- Have my goals or time horizons changed?
- Are my investment selections still appropriate?
- Am I on track to meet my objectives?
- What mistakes have I made, and how can I avoid them in the future?

### Investment Journal
Documenting decisions and outcomes improves self-awareness and learning.

**Journal Elements:**
- Investment decisions and rationale
- Emotional state at time of decision
- Expected outcomes and time frames
- Actual results and analysis of differences
- Lessons learned for future decisions

**Benefits:**
- Creates accountability for decisions
- Helps identify patterns in behavior
- Reduces hindsight bias
- Accelerates learning from experience

### Continuous Education
Ongoing learning helps avoid mistakes and improve investment outcomes.

**Education Focus Areas:**
- Investment principles and evidence-based strategies
- Behavioral finance and psychological biases
- Market history and cycles
- Tax-efficient investing techniques
- New investment vehicles and approaches

**Learning Resources:**
- Books by respected authors
- Academic research papers
- High-quality financial publications
- Investment courses and webinars
- Professional designations (if appropriate)

## Key Takeaways
- Market timing is virtually impossible to execute successfully and typically reduces returns
- Chasing recent performance leads to buying high and selling low across investments
- Investment costs compound over time and can significantly reduce long-term wealth
- Proper diversification reduces risk without necessarily sacrificing returns
- Panic selling during market downturns locks in losses and misses subsequent recoveries
- A systematic, disciplined approach helps avoid common investing mistakes
- Written plans, regular reviews, and ongoing education improve investment outcomes
- Recognizing and learning from mistakes is essential for long-term investment success
