# 1.6 Stocks: Ownership in Companies

## Introduction
Stocks represent one of the most fundamental and widely recognized investment vehicles in the financial world. When you purchase a stock, you're not just buying a piece of paper or a digital entry—you're acquiring partial ownership in an actual business. Understanding how stocks work, their various types, and how to interpret stock market information is essential for any investor looking to build wealth over time.

## What Stocks Represent

### Ownership Stake
When you buy a stock, you become a shareholder—a partial owner of the company. This ownership gives you certain rights, including:

- **Voting Rights**: The ability to vote on major corporate decisions and board of director elections (for common stock)
- **Claim on Assets**: A claim on a portion of the company's assets and earnings
- **Limited Liability**: Your financial responsibility is limited to your investment amount

### Legal Structure
Stocks exist because of the corporate structure, which allows companies to:
- Raise capital by selling ownership shares
- Operate as a separate legal entity from its owners
- Provide limited liability to shareholders

### Share Value Relationship
The value of a share is theoretically tied to:
- The company's current assets and liabilities
- Future earnings potential
- Growth prospects
- Market perception and sentiment

### Stock Certificates
Historically, stock ownership was represented by physical certificates. Today, most stocks exist in electronic form through:
- **Direct Registration System (DRS)**: Shares registered directly with the company
- **Street Name Registration**: Shares held by your broker on your behalf

## Common vs. Preferred Stocks

### Common Stock
Common stock is the most prevalent form of stock ownership and represents the basic ownership unit in a corporation.

**Characteristics:**
- **Voting Rights**: Typically one vote per share on corporate matters
- **Dividend Payments**: Not guaranteed, but paid at the discretion of the board of directors
- **Capital Appreciation**: Potential for price increases over time
- **Claim on Assets**: Last in line during bankruptcy or liquidation
- **Unlimited Upside**: No cap on potential returns if the company succeeds

**Examples:**
Most publicly traded stocks are common stocks, such as Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT), and Amazon (AMZN).

### Preferred Stock
Preferred stock is a hybrid security with characteristics of both stocks and bonds.

**Characteristics:**
- **Limited or No Voting Rights**: Typically cannot vote on corporate matters
- **Fixed Dividend Payments**: Predetermined dividend rate, similar to bond interest
- **Dividend Priority**: Paid before common stockholders
- **Higher Claim on Assets**: Stand ahead of common stockholders in bankruptcy
- **Potential for Callable Features**: Company may have the right to repurchase shares
- **Convertibility**: Some preferred shares can be converted to common stock
- **Limited Upside**: Price appreciation typically more limited than common stock

**Examples:**
Many financial institutions issue preferred stock, such as Bank of America Preferred Series L (BAC-L) or Wells Fargo Preferred Series Q (WFC-Q).

### Comparison Table: Common vs. Preferred Stock

| Feature | Common Stock | Preferred Stock |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Voting Rights | Yes | Limited or None |
| Dividends | Variable, not guaranteed | Fixed, prioritized |
| Dividend Growth | Can increase over time | Usually fixed |
| Price Appreciation Potential | Unlimited | Limited |
| Claim on Assets | Last | Before common, after bonds |
| Risk Level | Higher | Moderate |
| Volatility | Higher | Lower |
| Investor Appeal | Growth-oriented investors | Income-oriented investors |

## How Stock Markets Work

### Primary vs. Secondary Markets
Stock markets operate in two distinct phases:

**Primary Market:**
- Where new securities are issued and sold to investors for the first time
- Includes Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) and additional stock issuances
- Proceeds go directly to the issuing company
- Typically facilitated by investment banks

**Secondary Market:**
- Where existing securities are bought and sold between investors
- No new capital goes to the issuing company
- Provides liquidity for investors
- Includes stock exchanges and over-the-counter markets

### Major Stock Exchanges
Stock exchanges are organized marketplaces where securities are traded according to standardized rules.

**United States:**
- **New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)**: The largest exchange by market capitalization
- **Nasdaq**: Known for technology companies and electronic trading
- **NYSE American**: Formerly the American Stock Exchange, focuses on small-cap companies

**International:**
- **Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE)**: Japan's primary exchange
- **London Stock Exchange (LSE)**: One of Europe's oldest exchanges
- **Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE)**: Mainland China's largest exchange
- **Euronext**: Pan-European exchange

### Market Participants
Various entities participate in stock markets:

- **Retail Investors**: Individual investors trading for personal accounts
- **Institutional Investors**: Organizations like pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies
- **Market Makers**: Firms that facilitate trading by maintaining buy and sell quotes
- **Brokers**: Intermediaries who execute trades on behalf of clients
- **Regulators**: Government agencies that oversee market operations (e.g., SEC in the US)

### Order Types
Investors can place various types of orders:

- **Market Order**: Buy or sell at the current market price
- **Limit Order**: Buy or sell only at a specified price or better
- **Stop Order**: Becomes a market order when a specified price is reached
- **Stop-Limit Order**: Becomes a limit order when a specified price is reached
- **Day Order**: Valid only for the current trading day
- **Good-Till-Canceled (GTC)**: Remains active until executed or canceled

### Market Hours and Trading Sessions
Most major exchanges operate on a defined schedule:

- **Regular Trading Hours**: Typically 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM local time
- **Pre-Market Trading**: Limited trading before regular hours
- **After-Hours Trading**: Limited trading after regular hours
- **Circuit Breakers**: Temporary trading halts during extreme volatility

## Reading Stock Quotes and Charts

### Basic Stock Quote Components
A standard stock quote includes:

- **Ticker Symbol**: Unique alphabetic identifier (e.g., AAPL for Apple)
- **Current Price**: The most recent trading price
- **Change**: Price change from previous close (absolute and percentage)
- **Volume**: Number of shares traded
- **Bid**: Highest price a buyer is willing to pay
- **Ask**: Lowest price a seller is willing to accept
- **Day Range**: Lowest and highest prices for the current day
- **52-Week Range**: Lowest and highest prices over the past year
- **Market Cap**: Total market value of all outstanding shares
- **P/E Ratio**: Price-to-earnings ratio
- **Dividend Yield**: Annual dividend as a percentage of share price
- **EPS**: Earnings per share

### Stock Chart Types
Various chart formats help visualize price movements:

**Line Charts:**
- Plot closing prices over time
- Simple and easy to read
- Less detailed than other chart types

**Bar Charts:**
- Show opening, high, low, and closing prices (OHLC)
- Each vertical bar represents a time period
- Provides more information than line charts

**Candlestick Charts:**
- Japanese method showing OHLC data
- Bodies show opening and closing prices
- Wicks show high and low prices
- Color-coded (typically green/white for up, red/black for down)
- Reveals market psychology and patterns

**Point and Figure Charts:**
- Focus on price movements without time scale
- Filter out minor price movements
- Emphasize significant price changes

### Technical Indicators
Charts often include technical indicators to aid analysis:

- **Moving Averages**: Average price over a specific time period
- **Relative Strength Index (RSI)**: Momentum oscillator measuring speed and change of price movements
- **Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)**: Trend-following momentum indicator
- **Bollinger Bands**: Volatility bands placed above and below a moving average
- **Volume**: Number of shares traded, indicating participation level

### Chart Patterns
Experienced traders look for patterns in charts:

- **Head and Shoulders**: Potential reversal pattern with three peaks
- **Double Top/Bottom**: Reversal patterns showing two peaks or valleys
- **Triangles**: Continuation patterns showing converging trendlines
- **Flags and Pennants**: Short-term continuation patterns
- **Cup and Handle**: Bullish continuation pattern resembling a teacup

## Dividends and Capital Appreciation

### Dividends
Dividends are distributions of a company's earnings to shareholders.

**Types of Dividends:**
- **Cash Dividends**: Direct cash payments to shareholders
- **Stock Dividends**: Additional shares given to existing shareholders
- **Special Dividends**: One-time distributions, often from extraordinary events
- **Property Dividends**: Distribution of assets other than cash (rare)

**Key Dividend Dates:**
- **Declaration Date**: When the company announces the dividend
- **Ex-Dividend Date**: The cutoff date for dividend eligibility
- **Record Date**: The date the company checks its records to determine shareholders
- **Payment Date**: When the dividend is actually paid

**Dividend Metrics:**
- **Dividend Yield**: Annual dividend divided by share price
- **Dividend Payout Ratio**: Percentage of earnings paid as dividends
- **Dividend Growth Rate**: Rate at which dividends increase over time
- **Dividend Coverage Ratio**: Earnings per share divided by dividend per share

### Capital Appreciation
Capital appreciation refers to the increase in a stock's market price over time.

**Drivers of Capital Appreciation:**
- **Earnings Growth**: Increasing profits often lead to higher stock prices
- **Revenue Growth**: Expanding sales can drive stock prices higher
- **Market Expansion**: Entering new markets or product categories
- **Industry Trends**: Positive sector developments
- **Technological Advances**: Innovation that improves competitive position
- **Cost Reductions**: Improved margins through efficiency
- **Share Repurchases**: Reducing outstanding shares increases earnings per share
- **Market Sentiment**: Overall investor optimism

**Measuring Capital Appreciation:**
- **Absolute Return**: Simple percentage change in price
- **Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)**: Annualized return over multiple years
- **Total Return**: Capital appreciation plus dividends
- **Risk-Adjusted Return**: Return relative to volatility (e.g., Sharpe ratio)

### Total Return: Dividends + Capital Appreciation
The total return of a stock combines both dividend income and price appreciation.

**Formula:**
Total Return = (Ending Price - Beginning Price + Dividends) / Beginning Price

**Example:**
- Beginning Price: $100
- Ending Price (1 year later): $110
- Dividends Received: $3
- Total Return: ($110 - $100 + $3) / $100 = 13%

### Dividend vs. Growth Stocks

**Dividend Stocks:**
- Established companies in mature industries
- Regular, reliable dividend payments
- Lower growth rates
- Examples: Utilities, consumer staples, established financials
- Appeal to income-oriented investors

**Growth Stocks:**
- Companies reinvesting profits for expansion
- Little or no dividends
- Higher expected growth rates
- Examples: Technology, biotech, emerging markets
- Appeal to investors seeking capital appreciation

## Stock Investment Strategies

### Buy and Hold
- Long-term ownership regardless of short-term fluctuations
- Based on fundamental company strength
- Reduces transaction costs and taxes
- Examples: Warren Buffett's approach to investing

### Value Investing
- Seeking stocks trading below intrinsic value
- Focus on fundamentals like P/E ratio, book value
- Requires patience and contrarian thinking
- Examples: Benjamin Graham, Warren Buffett

### Growth Investing
- Focusing on companies with above-average growth potential
- Often accepts higher valuations for growth prospects
- Emphasizes revenue and earnings growth rates
- Examples: Peter Lynch, Philip Fisher

### Dividend Investing
- Prioritizing stocks with strong dividend yields
- Focus on dividend growth and sustainability
- Often favors established companies
- Examples: Utilities, REITs, consumer staples

### Index Investing
- Buying a broad market index rather than individual stocks
- Provides instant diversification
- Lower costs than active management
- Examples: S&P 500 index funds, total market funds

## Key Takeaways
- Stocks represent partial ownership in a company
- Common stocks offer voting rights and unlimited upside potential
- Preferred stocks provide more reliable income but limited appreciation
- Stock markets include both primary markets (new issues) and secondary markets (trading)
- Stock quotes and charts provide essential information for investment decisions
- Total return combines both dividends and capital appreciation
- Different investment strategies suit different investor goals and risk tolerances
